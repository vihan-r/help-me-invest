# Help Me Invest — Brand Guidelines

*The visual identity system. Every design decision — wordmark, colour, typography, photography, information design, layout, voice-in-design, and applied surfaces — is specified here. The document is the source of truth. If a piece of design can't be justified by something in this document, it doesn't ship.*

---

## Section 1 — Design Principles

*The five north-star principles that govern every visual decision HMI makes. Each one is action-guiding — a designer should be able to look at any draft and audit it against any principle and get a clear answer. Each one is specific to HMI — a principle that could apply to any brand isn't a principle, it's a platitude. Every later section of these guidelines, and every piece of design produced under this brand, should be auditable against these five.*

*These principles are the visual equivalent of Section 8 of the Brand Narrative Bible. Where the narrative voice rule says "make the claim, then back it," the design rules here say "make the page do one thing, then let it breathe." The voice and the design are doing the same work, in different mediums.*

---

### Principle 1 — The brand earns trust by making things simpler, not by making them more polished.

HMI does not perform competence through visual effort. It demonstrates competence by needing less. The brand earns trust the same way the narrative does — by saying one thing clearly, at the right size, with nothing around it. Polish is what brands add when they don't trust their content to stand on its own. HMI trusts its content.

**Consequences:**
- Whitespace is a primary design element, not a leftover.
- Every page, every screen, every social asset does one job. If it's doing three, we redesign.
- We resist the urge to "fill the space." Empty space signals confidence; crowded space signals a brand that's trying to convince you.
- No decorative flourishes, gradients, glows, drop shadows on type, or design elements added "to make it pop." If a sentence or image isn't strong enough to stand alone, the answer is to make it stronger — not to dress it.

**The test:** *Could we remove 30% of what's on this surface without losing the message?* If yes, we usually should.

---

### Principle 2 — Help is offered, never imposed. Every interaction leaves the customer in charge of the decision.

The narrative bible commits HMI to standing alongside the customer, never in front. The design system has to embody that relationship at every surface. The customer is the leader of their own property journey. HMI provides the knowledge, the access, and the trusted experts — but the choice, the timing, and the commitment always belong to the customer. The interface is the visual proof of this commitment.

**Consequences:**
- No high-pressure UX patterns: no countdown timers, no fake scarcity, no "only 3 left," no "23 people are looking at this right now."
- No pre-checked consent boxes, no dark patterns, no opt-outs hidden in fine print.
- Help is presented as an option, not as the path. CTAs say "see how this works" or "start here," not "claim your spot" or "don't miss out."
- The customer can always go back, change their mind, or proceed without the help being offered. The architecture supports the customer's autonomy at every step.
- Partner introductions are framed as invitations, not as recommendations the customer needs to follow. The customer evaluates the partner; the partner does not evaluate the customer.

**The test:** *If a customer left this page without taking the action we're suggesting, would they still feel respected and informed?* If no, the page is pushing — redesign so it offers instead.

---

### Principle 3 — Refuse the visual vocabulary of traditional property marketing — gold, navy, drone shots, suited handshakes, glass towers.

If the narrative bible commits HMI to refusing the *language* of the chain (financial freedom, savvy investor, exclusive opportunity), the design system must refuse the *visuals* of the chain with equal discipline. Every visual trope that signals "traditional property marketing" is an asset for them and a liability for us. This principle names the prohibitions plainly so there's no ambiguity about what's off-limits.

**Consequences:**
- No gold. No royal blue or corporate navy. No italic-serif "premium" wordmarks.
- No drone shots of suburbs or skylines. No aerial property photography.
- No suited professionals shaking hands. No glass-and-chrome offices. No boardroom imagery.
- No "lifestyle aspiration" photography (couple looking out a window, family on a lawn, woman holding coffee on a balcony, keys-in-hand, sold-sign imagery).
- No property exteriors as hero imagery — ever. Property is not what HMI sells. Capability is.

**The test:** *Could this design appear on the website of a traditional property marketing operator?* If yes, push it further until the answer is no.

---

### Principle 4 — Make every fee, every source, and every decision visible — even when not legally required.

The narrative bible's transparency commitment ("show everything") has a visual obligation. HMI shows what the chain hides — pricing, incentives, how value moves through the system. The design has to make that information *legible*, not just *present*. When HMI shows a fee structure, the design has to make it readable, not impressive. When HMI explains how a partner is paid, the breakdown has to be the clearest version of that explanation that exists anywhere — not the prettiest.

**Consequences:**
- Fee breakdowns are designed to be audited, not consumed. Clean column alignment, every number visible, every source cited.
- Source citations appear on every page that includes a number, fact, or claim — set in Warm Mid-Grey at body small size if structural, in Emerald at body size if the customer should verify it themselves.
- No "click to reveal" tricks where the disclosure is buried. If something matters, it's visible by default.
- Partner payment arrangements, platform incentive structures, and pricing rationale are documented openly on the platform — not just in legal disclosures.
- Information design is a primary brand asset, not a secondary craft. We invest in diagrams the way other brands invest in photography.

**The test:** *Would a customer who's been burned before look at this and feel they can verify what's being said?* If no, push the transparency further.

---

### Principle 5 — The customer's question, decision, or next step is the hero of every surface. The brand isn't.

The narrative bible is explicit: the customer is the hero of every story HMI tells. The design system has to honour that. The brand's visual identity is the architecture beneath the customer's experience — never the protagonist of it. HMI is not "look at us." HMI is "we built this for you." This principle names what *specifically* about the customer is the hero — not the customer's face or their imagined lifestyle, but the actual content of their engagement with the platform.

**Consequences:**
- The wordmark is small, confident, and used sparingly. The brand never repeats its own name decoratively across surfaces.
- The hero of any page is the customer's question, the customer's decision, or the customer's next step — not a brand statement.
- Customer-story content gets editorial-grade treatment. Brand-about-itself content gets minimal treatment.
- Photography centres real customers and real partners, never abstract brand iconography or "HMI as concept" imagery.
- We do not create "celebrating our own milestones" content. The brand's success is the customer's success — visualised through them, not through us.

**The test:** *In this design, who is the subject? If the answer is HMI, redesign so the answer is the customer.*

---

### How these principles relate to each other

The five principles function as a system, not a list. They reinforce each other:

- Principle 1 (restraint) is *how* HMI is confident.
- Principle 2 (help offered, never imposed) is *how* HMI honours the customer's autonomy.
- Principle 3 (refuse the category's visual vocabulary) is *how* HMI is recognisable.
- Principle 4 (show every fee and source) is *how* HMI is trustworthy.
- Principle 5 (customer's question as hero) is *how* HMI stays disciplined about who the brand is for.

A design that satisfies one but violates another is off-brand. A homepage that's beautifully restrained (P1) but centres HMI's logo and tagline above all else (violates P5) is off-brand. A fee breakdown that's transparent (P4) but uses gold and navy styling (violates P3) is off-brand. A partner introduction page that's well-designed (P1) but uses urgency language to push the customer forward (violates P2) is off-brand.

When in doubt, the principles are read in order — Principle 1 is the most fundamental, Principle 5 the most context-specific. But none of the five is optional.

## Section 2 — Logo and Wordmark

*The logo system is the most-repeated piece of brand asset HMI owns. It appears on every email signature, every social avatar, every footer, every partner deck, every favicon. The decisions in this section are deliberately conservative — because the logo's job is to be quietly correct everywhere, not to be loud anywhere.*

*The system has two marks: a primary wordmark and a compact mark. Both are typographic. Both are drawn from the same typeface family. There is no abstract symbol, no icon, and no metaphor in the identity. The wordmark and the compact mark are one identity at two sizes — not two separate logos.*

---

### 2a — The primary mark: the wordmark

The wordmark is the brand 95% of the time. It appears in every context where the full three words can be read clearly: website headers and footers, marketing materials, decks, email signatures, partner-facing documents, advertising, founder posts.

**Form:**
- The three words "help me invest" set in the brand's primary humanist sans (Plus Jakarta Sans, specified in Section 3).
- All lowercase. Lowercase puts the wordmark in the same conversational register as the brand's voice — plain-spoken, adult-to-adult, stating rather than announcing. Sentence case reads as a corporate proper noun; uppercase reads as an institution. Lowercase reads as a brand confident enough to write the way it talks.
- Set on a single line as the default. Stacked variants are available where horizontal space is constrained, but the single-line lockup is the canonical form.

**Construction:**
- "help me" is set in a heavier weight than "invest" — the weight differentiation puts the customer's imperative ("help me") forward and lets "invest" anchor it. This is a single, deliberate typographic decision that gives the wordmark its signature without adding any decorative element.
- "help me" is Plus Jakarta Sans **Bold**. "invest" is Plus Jakarta Sans **Medium**. The weight step is visible — the wordmark carries confident presence on every surface where it appears.
- The wordmark should read as one continuous statement, with the imperative felt forward.
- Letter spacing, word spacing, and proportions are fixed and specified in the master files. The wordmark is never re-typed; it is always used as an artwork file.

**Why this construction:**
- It honours the strength of the name (Principle 1 — restraint).
- The lowercase casing matches the brand's plain-spoken voice and the customer-led posture of the narrative (Principle 5 — the customer's question, decision, or next step is the hero, not the brand announcing itself in capitals).
- The weight differentiation dramatises that posture without saying a word — design doing the work the name already does.
- It refuses every visual instinct of the category (Principle 3) — no symbol, no italic-serif "premium" cue, no abstract corporate mark, no announcing-itself capitalisation.
- It is quietly built, not loud.

---

### 2b — The compact mark: "hmi"

The compact mark is used only where the full wordmark cannot be read clearly. Its job is to extend the brand's reach into spaces the wordmark cannot fit — not to compete with the wordmark for attention.

**Form:**
- The three letters "hmi" — lowercase — set in Plus Jakarta Sans Bold, in the same weight as "help me" in the wordmark.
- Lowercase only. Uppercase "HMI" reads as a corporate monogram (BBC, JPM, EY) and imports exactly the institutional cue the brand is built to refuse. Lowercase reads warmer, more modern, and more on-brand.
- No frame, no enclosure, no shape behind it. The mark is the three letters themselves.

**Where it's used:**
- Favicons.
- App icons.
- Social media avatars.
- Watermarks on partner-facing material.
- Any context where the full wordmark would render too small to be legible.

**Where it's not used:**
- Never alongside the wordmark. If the wordmark is present, the compact mark is not. They are the same identity at different sizes — not two elements used together.
- Never as a decorative graphic element in marketing copy or layout. The compact mark replaces the wordmark; it does not supplement it.
- Never re-typed inline in body copy. When the brand is referenced in writing, the proper name is "Help Me Invest" or, in informal contexts, "HMI" set in the standard body typeface — not the compact mark as a typographic element.

---

### 2c — Clear space and minimum sizes

The wordmark and compact mark both require breathing room. Crowded logos signal a brand trying too hard; logos with space around them signal a brand that doesn't need to.

**Clear space:**
- Around the wordmark: a minimum margin equal to the height of the lowercase "e" in "help" on all sides. No other text, image, or graphic element may enter that clear space.
- Around the compact mark: a minimum margin equal to the height of the "h" on all sides.

**Minimum sizes:**
- Wordmark: 120px wide (digital) / 30mm wide (print). Below this size, switch to the compact mark.
- Compact mark: 16px (digital) / 8mm (print). Below this size, the mark is not used.

These are minimums, not targets. In most applications the mark should be larger than the minimum — but never so large that it competes with the page's primary content for attention (Principle 5).

---

### 2d — Colour application

The wordmark and compact mark have three permitted colourways:

1. **Emerald on Soft Paper ground** — the default. Used wherever possible.
2. **Soft Paper on Emerald ground** — the inverse. Used on dark backgrounds, photography overlays, and contexts requiring contrast against a coloured ground.
3. **Warm Mid-Grey on Soft Paper ground** — a reduced version used for embossing, single-colour print, partner co-branding, and contexts requiring restraint.

The wordmark is never set in the accent colour. The accent is reserved for typographic emphasis and selective design moments — not for the logo itself.

The wordmark is never multi-colour. "help me" and "invest" are always the same colour, even though they are different weights.

---

### 2e — What the logo is not, and what it never does

A short list of permanent prohibitions. These exist to protect the system from the kind of drift that compounds over years.

- The logo does not contain an abstract symbol, icon, or graphic device.
- The logo is not modified, restyled, or redrawn for special occasions, campaigns, or partner co-marketing. The wordmark is the same wordmark in every context.
- The logo is not animated for the sake of animation. If the wordmark moves, the movement has a functional reason (e.g. a logo reveal in a video) — not a decorative one.
- The logo is not set in italics, outlined, embossed, or given drop shadows.
- The logo is not stretched, condensed, or rotated.
- The full wordmark is not used as a graphic pattern, repeated across a surface, or used decoratively in layouts.
- The compact mark is not used as a "branded element" inside body copy or marketing assets — only as a replacement for the wordmark in compact contexts.

If any of these rules need to be broken for a specific application, the decision goes back to the brand owner — not to the designer on the project.

---

### 2f — Files and governance

Master files are produced in:
- SVG (primary digital)
- PNG at standard sizes (web fallback)
- PDF / EPS (print)
- Single-colour variants for each colourway

All files live in a single source-of-truth folder. The wordmark is never re-typed by anyone — it is always used as a file. If a new application requires a logo variant that doesn't exist, the variant is added to the master folder, not improvised in the project file.

## Section 3 — Typography

*Typography is HMI's most-used brand asset. Every word the brand says, in every medium, lives inside the typeface system. The decisions in this section are foundational — every later section of these guidelines, and every piece of design produced under this brand, will be calibrated to the type. If the type is wrong, nothing else can save the brand. If the type is right, a lot of other decisions become forgiving.*

*The system is built on a single typeface, doing everything. The architecture is deliberately simple: one humanist sans handles every typographic role — headlines, body, UI, data, metadata, citations, and structural annotations. There is no secondary typeface, no serif, no monospace. The discipline is fewer decisions, made more confidently.*

---

### 3a — The typeface system

**The brand uses one typeface — Plus Jakarta Sans.**

A modern humanist sans serif with warm, rounded letterforms. Used for the wordmark, all headlines, all body copy, all UI, all marketing materials, all founder content, and all data and metadata roles. This is the brand's voice made visible, in every context where the brand has a voice.

Plus Jakarta Sans sits in the warm-modern-platform register — confident enough to refuse the chain's italic-serif premium cues, warm enough to refuse fintech's clinical coldness. The letterforms have a softness in the lowercase "a," "g," "e," and "o" that makes the typeface feel spoken rather than engineered. The weight range is generous, which the wordmark, the hierarchy system, and the metadata treatments all rely on.

**Why one typeface, not two.**
Earlier versions of this system used a secondary monospace for data, citations, and structural annotations. The monospace was doing real work — signalling "this is verifiable" through visual contrast with the sans. But monospaces carry inherited DNA from code editors and terminals, and that DNA was bleeding into surfaces that shouldn't feel statistical. A modern brand can signal verifiability through *position*, *colour*, and *layout discipline* without needing a separate typeface to announce it. One typeface, used precisely, is more restrained than two — and restraint is the brand's signature.

**No serif. No monospace.** The brand has no second typeface of any kind. The editorial gravitas a serif would provide can be achieved through sans handled at scale. The "raw data" quality a monospace would provide can be achieved through tighter layout, smaller sizes, and the Warm Mid-Grey colour. Both alternatives would add visual weight that contradicts Principle 1 (restraint).

**Source.** Plus Jakarta Sans is available on Google Fonts and via npm, free under the SIL Open Font License. The brand uses the official-distributed files only. No re-cuts, no modifications, no derivatives.

---

### 3b — How the wordmark is constructed

The wordmark "help me invest" is set in Plus Jakarta Sans with a specific construction that does not change.

- **"help me"** is set in Plus Jakarta Sans **Bold**.
- **"invest"** is set in Plus Jakarta Sans **Medium**.
- The two words and one phrase are set on a single line with consistent letter spacing and word spacing.
- All three words are lowercase.
- The weight differentiation puts the customer-led imperative forward, with "invest" anchoring it. The wordmark reads as one continuous statement, with confident presence on every surface.

The wordmark is never re-typed by hand. It is always used as a master artwork file. If the wordmark needs to be reproduced at a new size or in a new format, the master file is used — not a fresh setting in Plus Jakarta Sans.

The compact mark "hmi" is set in Plus Jakarta Sans **Bold**, lowercase, with no weight differentiation across the three letters.

---

### 3c — Headline and display hierarchy

Hierarchy in the brand is built primarily through **size**, secondarily through **weight**, and through colour only in two specific cases: headlines are set in Emerald (the primary brand colour), and a single strategic phrase per surface may be highlighted on a Mint background (the brand's signature typographic move, governed by the rules in Section 4d). Outside those two cases, colour is never used to create emphasis within type — emphasis is built through scale.

All headline sizes below are screen-first specifications. Print sizes scale proportionally.

**Display (D1)** — *for hero statements, marketing campaign frames, and the largest brand moments only.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold
- 72–96px on desktop / 48–60px on mobile
- Line height: 1.05 (1.2 if the headline contains a Mint phrase highlight, to prevent the highlight on one line from overlapping content on adjacent lines)
- Letter spacing: -0.02em
- Used sparingly. One display headline per page maximum.

**Headline 1 (H1)** — *primary page headline.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold
- 48–56px on desktop / 36–40px on mobile
- Line height: 1.1
- Letter spacing: -0.015em

**Headline 2 (H2)** — *section headline.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold
- 32–36px on desktop / 26–28px on mobile
- Line height: 1.15
- Letter spacing: -0.01em

**Headline 3 (H3)** — *subsection headline.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold
- 24–28px on desktop / 22–24px on mobile
- Line height: 1.2
- Letter spacing: -0.005em

**Headline 4 (H4)** — *minor headline, card titles, list section titles.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold
- 18–20px
- Line height: 1.3
- Letter spacing: 0

Headlines are set in SemiBold — not Medium, not Bold. SemiBold carries the brand at the right register: declarative enough to claim the space a headline is allowed to claim, restrained enough to refuse the loud product-marketing register that Bold would impose. Bold and ExtraBold are not used for headlines anywhere in the system. The wordmark's Bold weight is the exception — a logo is allowed to carry more weight than the rest of the system, and the Bold/Medium differentiation between "help me" and "invest" is what makes the wordmark structurally distinct from running type.

Headlines are set in **sentence case** — *capital first letter, lowercase everything else*. This is the one place in the system where lowercase is not the default. Sentence case in headlines preserves readability while keeping the brand's plain-spoken register. All-lowercase headlines feel mannered at the size headlines run; all-caps headlines feel institutional.

The wordmark is the exception that proves the rule — it is always all-lowercase because it is a logo, not a sentence.

---

### 3d — Body copy

Body copy is where the brand's voice does its most consistent work. Every voice rule from Section 8 of the Brand Narrative Bible — make the claim, then back it; one idea per sentence; verbs of agency over adjectives of aspiration — relies on body typography that respects them. Body copy is set in **Warm Charcoal (`#1A2B22`)** — the brand's reading colour, not its identity colour. The brand recedes here so the words can be read at length.

**Body (default)**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 16–18px on desktop / 16px on mobile
- Colour: Warm Charcoal (`#1A2B22`)
- Line height: 1.6
- Letter spacing: 0
- Maximum line length: 65–75 characters. Longer lines fatigue the reader and contradict the "one idea per sentence" voice rule.

**Body emphasis (within paragraphs)**
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold for emphasis, in the same Warm Charcoal as the surrounding body. Used sparingly — one or two emphasised phrases per paragraph maximum.
- Emphasis is created through weight, not colour. Body-copy emphasis is never set in Emerald or Mint — switching the brand colours into running text breaks the calm reading surface.
- Italics are not used for emphasis in body copy. Italics are reserved for proper nouns of works (book titles, publication names) and for genuinely emphatic phrasing in editorial contexts. Decorative italics are never used.

**Inline links within body copy**
- Set in Warm Charcoal (same as the surrounding body), with a 1px Warm Charcoal underline at ~3px offset.
- On hover, the underline thickens to 2px and shifts to Emerald, signalling interactivity without switching the link colour into the body stream.
- Links never use Emerald as their default text colour — keeping body uniform makes long-form reading comfortable, and the underline carries the link signal cleanly.

**Body large** — *for opening paragraphs, lede sentences, or content that needs slightly more presence.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 20–22px
- Colour: Warm Charcoal
- Line height: 1.5

**Body small** — *for captions, footnotes, and contextual annotations.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 14px
- Colour: Warm Charcoal (or Warm Mid-Grey for purely structural metadata like slide numbers, dates, eyebrows)
- Line height: 1.5

**Fine print and legal**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 12–13px
- Colour: Warm Charcoal, never lightened to grey to be visually de-emphasised.
- Line height: 1.5
- Used for footers, terms, disclosures. Fine print is fine in size, not in importance — and the brand never visually buries information it is contractually obliged to disclose. This is Principle 4 (make every fee, every source, and every decision visible) applied to the smallest text in the system.

**One important exception — body copy on Emerald grounds.**
When body copy appears on an Emerald background (partner decks, dark hero sections, reverse-out contexts), it is set in Soft Paper (`#F6F7F4`), not Warm Charcoal. Warm Charcoal is too close to Emerald in temperature to read well against it. The rule is: on light grounds, body is Warm Charcoal; on Emerald grounds, body is Soft Paper.

---

### 3e — UI typography

UI elements have their own specifications because their job is different from body or headline copy — they need to be unambiguous, scannable, and tap-targetable.

**Buttons**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium
- 14–16px
- Letter spacing: 0
- Button labels are verbs and short phrases. "Read this," "See the breakdown," "Start here." Not "Learn More" or "Get Started" (Principle 5; voice rules in Section 8 of the narrative bible).
- Primary button label colour: Soft Paper (on Emerald fill). Secondary button label colour: Emerald (on Lighter Mint fill). See Section 4f for the full button hierarchy.

**Form labels**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium
- 14px
- Form labels are plain questions. "What's your first name?" not "First Name *".

**Form inputs**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 16px (minimum — never smaller on mobile, to prevent zoom-on-focus)

**Navigation**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium
- 14–16px

**Tags, badges, metadata**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium
- 12–13px
- Set in sentence case or lowercase, never all-caps. All-caps tags signal "fintech" or "ecommerce" — both wrong registers for HMI.

---

### 3f — Data, metadata, and structural annotations

Since the brand uses one typeface, data and metadata roles that would traditionally use a monospace are handled by Plus Jakarta Sans treated deliberately. The visual signal of "this is verifiable, not marketing" is carried through layout discipline and colour — not through a different typeface.

**The recipe for any data or metadata role:**

1. **Smaller size than the surrounding body.** Body small (14px) or fine print (12–13px), depending on context.
2. **Warm Mid-Grey (`#8B8881`) for purely structural metadata** — slide numbers, timestamps, version labels, internal references. The grey signals "this is page furniture, not content."
3. **Emerald for any data the reader needs to verify** — fee percentages, partner credentials, source citations. The brand never lightens verifiable information.
4. **Tabular layout with clean column alignment** for any numerical content. Right-aligned numbers, left-aligned labels, consistent column widths. Layout does the work that monospace previously did.
5. **Plus Jakarta Sans Medium** for emphasis within data tables — never SemiBold or Bold (which would read as marketing).

**Specific contexts:**

- **Fee breakdowns and pricing tables.** Set in Plus Jakarta Sans Medium for column headers, Regular for values. Numbers right-aligned in their columns. Source and date appear at the bottom of the table in Body Small, in Warm Mid-Grey if structural, in Emerald if the data itself is the point.

- **Source citations.** "As at 23 May 2026" or "Source: APRA, Q2 2025" — set in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular, Body Small size, in Warm Mid-Grey. Always present where data is shown; never hidden.

- **Partner credentials and verifiable identifiers.** ABNs, licence numbers, regulatory references — set in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular at body size, in Emerald, never lightened. These are content the reader can audit.

- **Slide numbers, version labels, document metadata.** Plus Jakarta Sans Medium, smallest size (10–12px), in Warm Mid-Grey. Page furniture.

**The discipline:** when reaching for a different typeface to make data "look like data," the right answer is to make the data clearer through layout instead. Smaller size, cleaner alignment, considered colour. The reader understands what's data and what's marketing through context, not through font choice.

---

### 3g — Behaviour and rules

Five operational rules that apply across the system.

1. **Hierarchy is built through size, not through colour or style.** All headlines are the same colour as body copy. Distinguishing a headline from a paragraph happens through scale, not through tinting the headline or rendering it in the accent colour.

2. **One typeface, used precisely.** The brand has no secondary typeface. Every surface uses Plus Jakarta Sans. The discipline isn't "sans plus mono where it earns its place" — it's "sans, used at the right weight, size, and colour for every role."

3. **Generous leading. Comfortable line length.** Body copy has a line height of 1.6 and a maximum line length of 65–75 characters. Crowded type contradicts the brand's voice rule of "one idea per sentence." Spacious type reinforces it.

4. **No decorative typographic treatments.** No drop shadows on type. No outlines. No gradients applied to letterforms. No stretching, condensing, or rotating. No "Word Art." If a sentence needs more visual weight, the answer is a larger size or a different placement — not a typographic effect.

5. **Type is left-aligned by default.** Centred type reads as ceremonial and institutional. Justified type reads as corporate and dated. Right-aligned type is reserved for specific tabular contexts (right-aligned numbers in a fee table, for example). The default — ragged-right, left-aligned — reads as plain-spoken and modern, which is exactly the register the brand needs.

---

### 3h — Loading and fallbacks

Plus Jakarta Sans is delivered via standard web font loading. The fallback stack is:

```
"Plus Jakarta Sans", -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", system-ui, sans-serif
```

Web font loading uses `font-display: swap` to prevent invisible text during load. Critical above-the-fold weights (Plus Jakarta Sans Regular, Medium, Bold) are preloaded. SemiBold is loaded as needed.

---

### 3i — Summary

The type system in one paragraph:

> Plus Jakarta Sans does everything. Set in sentence case, left-aligned, with generous line height and comfortable line lengths. Hierarchy is built through size — SemiBold for all headlines (Display through H4) and Regular for body. The colour split is the discipline: headlines are set in Emerald (the brand announces itself), body copy is set in Warm Charcoal (the brand recedes for reading). One deliberate exception to the "no colour-based hierarchy" rule — a single strategic phrase per surface, drawn from the brand's locked vocabulary, may be highlighted on a Mint background. Data and metadata roles (fee tables, citations, slide numbers, structural annotations) are handled by Plus Jakarta Sans at smaller sizes, with layout discipline and colour doing the work that a monospace would otherwise do — Warm Mid-Grey for structural metadata, Emerald for verifiable data. The wordmark "help me invest" sets "help me" in Plus Jakarta Sans Bold and "invest" in Plus Jakarta Sans Medium, all lowercase, on a single line. The compact mark "hmi" is set in Plus Jakarta Sans Bold, lowercase. One typeface, used precisely.

## Section 4 — Colour Palette

*Colour is the second-most-recognisable brand signal after type, and the first thing the eye registers before reading anything. The palette is deliberately small: six colours, six roles, no expansion. Every additional colour would be another decision a designer could get wrong. The discipline is fewer colours, used confidently — with one clean exception baked into the system: the Mint phrase highlight, which is the brand's signature typographic move.*

*The palette is built around two greens that do different emotional work. A deep emerald carries the trust and gravitas the brand needs as a financial education platform. A soft mint carries the warmth and approachability the brand needs to feel like it's on the customer's side. The two greens are a conversation between expertise and approachability — the same brand at two emotional temperatures.*

*The palette refuses three category conventions: the gold-and-navy of traditional Australian property, the electric-blue-and-black of modern fintech, and the green-and-clay earthy register of wellness-adjacent brands. HMI is none of these. The palette signals as much before the type even loads.*

### 4a — The six-role colour system

The brand has six colours, each with one specific role. The roles are fixed.

| Role | Name | Hex | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ground** | Soft Paper | `#F6F7F4` | All backgrounds. 65-75% of any surface. |
| **Soft surface · gentle** | Lighter Mint | `#D4E8B5` | Card backgrounds, content groupings, hover states, gentle tonal lift. |
| **Soft surface · warm** | Mint | `#A8D5B4` | Full soft sections, partner panels, the strategic phrase highlight. |
| **Primary** | Emerald | `#0A4B34` | Wordmark, headlines, primary UI, primary buttons, accent surfaces. |
| **Body Ink** | Warm Charcoal | `#1A2B22` | Body copy, secondary text, inline links. The reading colour. |
| **Neutral** | Warm Mid-Grey | `#8B8881` | Dividers, borders, structural greys, page furniture metadata. |

That is the entire system.

**Two greens + two soft surfaces + one body ink.** The brand's two-greens identity is unchanged — Emerald carries the trust, Mint carries the warmth, and the conversation between them is the brand's signature visual move. Lighter Mint is not a third expressive green; it's a *softer* version of the Mint surface role, used where a section needs gentle lift rather than full warmth. Warm Charcoal is not an expressive colour at all — it sits in the same family as the greens (it's a very dark warm green-black, not a neutral grey) but it recedes so the type can be read at length. The greens *announce*; the charcoal *reads*.

**There is no accent colour.** Emphasis happens through scale, position, and — in one deliberate exception — through the Mint phrase highlight (see Section 4d). Lighter Mint is a *surface* colour, not an accent — it gives the brand finer tonal control across pages without introducing a contrasting hue.

### 4b — Ground: Soft Paper (#F6F7F4)

The dominant background colour. Present on every surface, behind every word, supporting every image. Soft Paper is the single highest-leverage decision in the palette — it's what separates HMI from 95% of the category at first glance.

**Rules:**
- Used as the default background on every surface.
- Never replaced with pure white "to brighten things up." Pure white is not in the palette.
- One exception: very large photographic surfaces (a hero image, a customer portrait) can extend full-bleed without Soft Paper around them. Soft Paper returns the moment the image ends.

**Why this specific tone:**
- Warm enough to refuse the fintech-clinical register that pure white imports.
- Restrained enough to refuse the editorial-rustic register that a more saturated cream would commit the brand to.
- Comfortable next to data, photography, both greens, and typography at every weight.

---

### 4c — Primary: Emerald (#0A4B34)

The brand's identity colour. Emerald is what announces HMI — the wordmark, the headlines, the primary buttons, the colour of every element where the brand is making itself visible. It is not the colour for reading; that's Warm Charcoal (Section 4e). Emerald is the colour for declaring.

Emerald is a deep, considered green that reads as trusted-expert finance — confident enough to anchor the whole system, modern enough to refuse the heritage-advisor register, distinctive enough to give the brand an instantly-recognisable colour identity.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- All wordmark and compact mark applications on Soft Paper grounds.
- All headlines and display type.
- **Primary button fills** (Emerald background, Soft Paper text) — used only when a surface has a single most-important action. See Section 4f for the button hierarchy. Most CTAs on the platform are not primary buttons; they are secondary buttons in Lighter Mint.
- Type colour on Lighter Mint and Mint surfaces — including secondary button labels and headlines that sit inside soft-section panels.
- Inline text links (rendered with an underline so the colour signal is reinforced by typography).
- Full-bleed surface colour for partner decks, founder content covers, and hero sections requiring reversed-out type.
- Structural elements of all diagrams — labelled boxes, connecting lines, and the markers that indicate where fees are taken.

Never used for:
- Body copy. Body copy is set in Warm Charcoal — the reading colour. Setting body in Emerald makes the brand colour fight for attention against itself across long paragraphs.
- Decoration or visual interest with no structural purpose.

**Never tinted, lightened, or used at reduced opacity.** If less visual weight is needed, the answer is the Neutral colour or a different colour role — not a lighter Emerald.

**Why this specific tone:**
- Modern emerald rather than forest green. The brand is a financial platform, not a nature brand. Forest greens read as earthy and outdoor; emerald reads as confident, current, and financial.
- Refuses every category convention: not navy (banking), not black (fintech), not gold (chain), not nature-coded forest (wellness platforms).
- Carries the trusted-expert lineage of financial green — money, growth, stability — without being heritage-advisor about it.
- Pairs naturally with Mint as a two-green system, where the relationship between the two does the brand's emotional work.

---

### 4d — Secondary: Mint (#A8D5B4)

The warmth and approachability colour. Mint does two specific jobs in the brand — both deliberate, both governed by strict rules.

**Job 1 — Soft-section surfaces.** Mint is the background colour for soft sections that need to feel inviting. Partner-introduction panels, sign-up surfaces, customer-welcome contexts, educational callouts, hover states. When a part of the page should feel softer and more welcoming than the surrounding Soft Paper, the background is Mint.

**Job 2 — The strategic phrase highlight.** Mint is the one exception to the brand's "hierarchy through size, not through colour" rule. A short phrase drawn from the brand's locked strategic vocabulary may be highlighted inline by setting it on a Mint background with rounded corners — visually picking out the brand's thesis within a longer headline. This is the brand's signature typographic move.

**Rules for the phrase highlight:**

- **Soft Paper grounds only.** The phrase highlight is *only* used on Soft Paper backgrounds. On Emerald grounds (covers, dark hero sections, partner deck covers, reverse-out contexts), the highlight is not used — Mint sits too close to Emerald in colour family for the highlight to land. Instead of pulling the eye, it recedes. On Emerald grounds, strategic phrase emphasis is achieved through line break and scale, not through colour.
- **One highlight per surface, maximum.** If the hero uses the highlight, nothing else on the page does. The discipline is what makes the move land.
- **Only on strategic brand vocabulary.** The phrase must come from a small, curated set of brand-anchored expressions — *"on your own terms," "stand alongside, not in front," "show every fee,"* and a small number of other locked phrases. Designers do not get to highlight whatever feels punchy. The vocabulary is fixed.
- **Only on hero and display-scale headlines.** Never in body copy. Never on UI labels. Never on buttons. Never on captions.
- **Visual treatment is fixed.** Mint background (`#A8D5B4`), Emerald text (`#0A4B34`) remains as the type colour, small rounded corners (~6px) on the highlight, no border, no shadow.
- **Padding must accommodate descenders.** The highlight is not a tight box around the x-height. It extends comfortably below the baseline and above the cap line so the type is never clipped. Specifically: vertical padding of roughly **0.25 × cap height** below the baseline (enough to clear the deepest descender of any letter — y, g, p, q, j — plus a small visual margin), and roughly **0.15 × cap height** above the cap line. Horizontal padding of roughly **0.3-0.4 × cap height** on each side of the first and last character. The visual reading should be: comfortable air around the type, with no letter forms ever touching or being clipped by the background edge.
- **The highlight is part of the type, not a sticker.** It sits on the same baseline, with the same letter-spacing, as the rest of the headline. The padding extends the background — it does not nudge the type up or alter its position.
- **Line-height consideration.** Headlines containing a phrase highlight need slightly more line-height than ordinary headlines (typically 1.2 instead of 1.1) to prevent the highlight on one line from overlapping the descender or cap of an adjacent line.

**Used for soft-section surfaces:**

- Soft section backgrounds where a part of the page should feel softer and more inviting than surrounding Soft Paper.
- Partner-introduction panels.
- Approachable UI contexts: sign-up panels, getting-started prompts, customer welcome surfaces, friendly callouts.
- Hover states on secondary buttons (Mint background, Emerald text).
- Educational content callouts where an explanation needs to feel different from surrounding body content.

**Never used for:**

- Body copy. Mint is a surface or a phrase highlight, never the colour of running text.
- The wordmark.
- Decoration or visual interest with no structural purpose.
- Buttons or UI elements that need to draw the eye to a primary action — those are Emerald.
- Arbitrary emphasis. The phrase highlight is reserved for the locked strategic vocabulary only.

**Why this specific tone:**

- Light enough to feel warm and welcoming, saturated enough to feel like a real brand colour rather than a faded accent.
- Pairs cleanly with Emerald — they read as the same brand at two emotional temperatures, not as contrasting colours.
- Refuses the earthy / nature-coded register that a clay or terracotta accent would impose.
- Refuses the wellness-app register that a pastel pink or peach would impose.
- Carries enough green DNA to feel coherent with Emerald, but enough lightness to clearly serve a different emotional purpose.

---

### 4e — Soft Surface · Gentle: Lighter Mint (#D4E8B5)

The third tonal layer. Lighter Mint is a softer, lifted version of the Mint surface — used where a section, card, or panel needs gentle lift from the Soft Paper ground without the full warmth of Mint. It is a *tonal* colour, not an expressive one. Where Mint is used to make a section feel inviting and warm, Lighter Mint is used to make a section feel *grouped* — visually separated from surrounding content with the lightest possible touch.

Lighter Mint also does one specific UI job: it is the brand's **secondary button colour** — the quieter half of the two-button system. The brand deliberately runs quiet CTAs because Principle 2 ("Help is offered, never imposed") rules out high-pressure visual treatments. A loud CTA is a brand that's pushing. HMI's brand offers.

Lighter Mint sits between Soft Paper and Mint on the same green family axis. The three together — Soft Paper, Lighter Mint, Mint — give the brand fine tonal control across long pages, allowing visual rhythm without breaking the calm. The discipline is that Lighter Mint never tries to do Mint's job, and Mint never tries to do Lighter Mint's job.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- **Secondary buttons** — Lighter Mint fill with Emerald text. The quieter CTA. Used when the action is genuinely optional, exploratory, or one of several equally weighted choices. Often paired with a primary Emerald button on the same surface, or used alone where the brand wants to offer rather than push.
- Card backgrounds within Soft Paper pages. Partner cards, content tiles, feature panels — places where grouped content needs to lift gently off the ground.
- Hover and selected states on Soft Paper surfaces. When a row, card, or section becomes interactive, it shifts to Lighter Mint as a soft acknowledgement.
- Quiet section dividers. Where a long page needs gentle tonal rhythm, sections of Lighter Mint can be alternated with Soft Paper to pace the reading.
- Educational callouts and quotes that should feel gathered but not warmly emphasised.
- Form field backgrounds in inviting contexts (sign-up forms, welcome flows), where a Mint background would feel too warm but Soft Paper would feel inert.

Never used for:
- The strategic phrase highlight. Lighter Mint sits too close to Soft Paper to do the highlight's job — the highlight would recede instead of emphasising. The phrase highlight is Mint-only.
- Headlines, body copy, or any running type. Lighter Mint is a surface colour only — when type appears *on* Lighter Mint (secondary button labels, card content), the type is set in Emerald or Warm Charcoal.
- The wordmark or compact mark.
- Backgrounds where full Mint warmth is required. If a section needs to feel actively warm (partner introductions, customer welcome moments, soft-section heroes), use Mint.

**The secondary button — detailed spec:**

Secondary buttons use:
- **Fill:** Lighter Mint (`#D4E8B5`)
- **Label:** Plus Jakarta Sans Medium, 14-16px, Emerald (`#0A4B34`)
- **Padding:** Same as primary buttons (approximately 11-14px vertical, 18-22px horizontal, scaled to button size)
- **Corner radius:** 10px (`rounded-hmi-md`), matching primary
- **Hover state:** Fill shifts to Mint (`#A8D5B4`) — the same surface family, one step warmer. Type colour stays Emerald.
- **Pressed state:** Fill shifts back to Lighter Mint with a slight inset shadow or border tightening.
- **Disabled state:** Fill reduces to Soft Paper, label reduces to Warm Mid-Grey, with a thin Warm Mid-Grey border.

The secondary button is *not* a "less important version" of the primary — it's the default. Most pages probably use a Lighter Mint button as the primary call, with the Emerald button reserved for the rarer moments when the page has a single most-important action. See Section 4f for the full button hierarchy.

**The relationship between Lighter Mint and Mint:**

Think of them as two different volume levels of the same idea. Mint is the deliberate warm moment — used selectively, on a specific section, when the brand wants the eye to land on something soft. Lighter Mint is the ambient warm layer — used more freely, across cards, buttons, and groupings, to give the brand tonal rhythm without making any one moment shout.

A page can comfortably use both. A homepage might have Soft Paper as the ground, Lighter Mint partner cards in one section, a Lighter Mint secondary button in the hero, and a full Mint soft-section panel in another. The eye reads the hierarchy: Lighter Mint = "this is grouped or offered"; Mint = "this is the warm moment."

**Why this specific tone:**

- Light enough to register as a soft tonal step from Soft Paper, not as a separate brand colour.
- Saturated enough to feel intentional rather than accidental — not so close to Soft Paper that it reads as a printing artefact.
- Pairs cleanly with both Soft Paper (as a lifted layer above it) and Mint (as the softer sibling alongside it). The three tones read as one tonal family.
- Provides comfortable contrast for both Warm Charcoal body text (approximately 11.3:1) and Emerald button labels (approximately 7.7:1) — meaning Lighter Mint surfaces can hold readable type without the colour fighting the background.

### 4f — The button hierarchy

The brand has three button styles, each with a specific role. The hierarchy is not "more or less important" — it's "what kind of call this surface is making."

**Primary button — Emerald fill, Soft Paper text.**
The strongest visual call on a page. Used for *the* one action the brand most wants the customer to take in a given context — and only used when the page genuinely has one such action. One per surface maximum.

Use for: "Start here" on the homepage hero, "Create your account" on sign-up flows, "Send to my partner" on partner-introduction screens, "Save" on settings-with-real-consequences. Anywhere the action is the page's single point of focus.

**Secondary button — Lighter Mint fill, Emerald text.**
The quieter CTA. Used when the action is exploratory, optional, or one of several equally weighted choices. Often paired with a primary button on the same page (e.g. "Start here" + "See how this works"). Frequently used *alone* on content surfaces where the brand wants to offer the action without pushing it.

Use for: "See how this works," "Read the breakdown," "Compare your options," "Browse partners," "Continue reading." Most CTA moments on the platform are secondary buttons, not primary.

**Tertiary text link — Warm Charcoal with underline, shifts to Emerald on hover.**
The quietest call. Used for non-action interactions — navigational links, "Learn more," "View details," "Back to all partners," inline references within body copy.

**The discipline:**

Designers audit a layout by asking two questions:

1. *Does this page have a single most-important action?* If yes, that action gets the Emerald button; nothing else on the page does. If no — if the page is exploratory, informational, or offering multiple equally-weighted paths — all CTAs are Lighter Mint.

2. *Is the loudest button the action the brand most wants?* If a less-important action is rendered in Emerald, the page is pushing the wrong thing. Demote it to Lighter Mint.

The brand defaults toward Lighter Mint, not Emerald. This is intentional — restraint as the brand's CTA register. Loud CTAs are a brand pushing; quiet CTAs are a brand offering. HMI is in the second category.

---

### 4g — Body Ink: Warm Charcoal (#1A2B22)

The reading colour. Warm Charcoal is the colour of every long-form sentence the customer encounters — body copy in paragraphs, secondary text in captions, lede paragraphs beneath headlines, the body of UI panels. It is not a brand-announcing colour. Its job is to recede enough that the type can be read at length without the brand colour fighting for attention against itself.

Warm Charcoal is a very dark warm green-black. It sits in the same family as Emerald (both share a warm green undertone) but its colour temperature pulls toward neutral so it reads as comfortable reading text rather than as the brand colour repeated.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- Body copy in every context — homepage, education content, partner introductions, founder posts, email body, anywhere paragraphs are read.
- Secondary text — captions, footnotes, supporting paragraphs.
- Body of UI panels and cards where the user is reading content, not interacting with controls.
- Inline links *within body copy* (rendered with an underline; the underline carries the link signal, not a colour change to Emerald, so the body remains comfortable to read).

Never used for:
- Headlines or display copy. Headlines are Emerald — they announce the brand.
- The wordmark. The wordmark is Emerald.
- Buttons or primary UI actions. Those are Emerald-filled with Soft Paper text.
- Anywhere the brand should *announce* itself rather than recede.

**Why this specific tone:**

- Dark enough to read comfortably at body sizes across long paragraphs.
- Warm enough to harmonise with the Emerald/Mint family — not a neutral grey, not a cool charcoal.
- Recessive enough that Emerald headlines and buttons read as the *announcements* on a page, with the body settling beneath them as readable content.
- Not pure black. Pure black is corporate, sterile, and out of step with the brand's warm-platform register.

**Why Warm Charcoal, not just lightened Emerald:**

A lighter Emerald would read as a tinted brand colour — the eye still sees it as "the brand colour, weaker." Warm Charcoal reads as a different role entirely: not the brand colour at all, but the colour of the words the customer is reading. The separation between announcement (Emerald) and reading (Warm Charcoal) is what gives long-form HMI surfaces their calm.

---

### 4h — Neutral: Warm Mid-Grey (#8B8881)

A single mid-grey with a warm undertone, used for genuine structural greyness — never for de-emphasising content.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- Hairline dividers and rules between sections.
- Form input borders and table borders.
- Disabled UI states.
- Secondary metadata labels (e.g. dates, timestamps, section eyebrows, slide numbers).
- Captions beneath data tables and source citations that are structural rather than verifiable (e.g. *"Source: APRA, Q2 2025"*).

Never used for:
- Body copy or "secondary" text. Secondary text is set in Warm Charcoal at a smaller size.
- Fine print, footnotes, or legal text. These are set in Warm Charcoal, not lightened. Fine print is fine in size, not in importance.
- De-emphasising any content the customer needs to read.

**Why a warm grey, not a cool one:**

- Cool greys (with blue undertones) read as fintech and clash with the warm ground.
- A grey with subtle warm undertones sits naturally next to Soft Paper, both greens, and Warm Charcoal — reading as part of the same family rather than as a clinical out-of-system colour.

---

### 4i — How the colours work together

The system is built so each colour has a clear lane. A well-designed HMI surface uses:

- Soft Paper as the ground (always).
- Emerald for the wordmark, headlines, and the rare primary button (always).
- Warm Charcoal for body copy and reading-length text (always).
- Lighter Mint as a gentle tonal lift for cards, content groupings, hover states, and most CTA buttons (frequently — wherever Soft Paper alone would feel flat, and on most action buttons).
- Mint for full soft-section surfaces and the strategic phrase highlight (selectively — used as a deliberate warm moment, not as ambient texture).
- Warm Mid-Grey for genuine structure only (rarely).

A surface that uses all six colours equally is over-designed. A well-balanced HMI surface might be 60% Soft Paper, 15% Warm Charcoal (body copy is most of the words), 8% Emerald (headlines, wordmark, buttons), 10% Lighter Mint (cards or content groupings), 6% Mint (a soft section or a single highlighted phrase), 1% Warm Mid-Grey. Those proportions are the discipline.

**The two-greens relationship.**

The brand's most distinctive visual move is the conversation between Emerald and Mint. When the two appear on the same surface — Emerald as the headline colour, Mint as a soft section behind it or as the highlight on a strategic phrase — the brand reads as both confident and warm at once. This pairing is the brand's signature use of colour. It should appear prominently on the homepage, in partner introductions, in onboarding flows, in any context where the brand wants to express both halves of its character at the same time. Warm Charcoal sits beneath the conversation as the calm reading layer; it does not participate in the two-greens move.

**The tonal layer (Soft Paper, Lighter Mint, Mint).**

The three tones form a tonal family that gives the brand rhythm across long pages. The discipline is hierarchy by intent: Soft Paper for reading, Lighter Mint for grouping, Mint for the deliberate warm moment. A page that uses all three creates visual pacing without breaking the calm. A page that uses one is restrained; a page that uses two is rhythmic; a page that uses all three is fully realised.

**Reverse contexts (Emerald as ground).**

When Emerald is used as a background — partner decks, hero sections, dark-mode UI — the relationships invert cleanly:
- Soft Paper becomes the headline and primary type colour.
- Mint continues to function as a softer-section surface, used inside Emerald panels to create a layered feel.
- Lighter Mint is used minimally on Emerald grounds — the contrast against Emerald is too soft to do useful tonal work, and the surface reads as muddied. Where a softer lift inside an Emerald section is needed, use Mint at full saturation instead.
- **The Mint phrase highlight is not used on Emerald grounds.** Mint and Emerald sit too close in colour family for the highlight to land — instead of pulling the eye, it recedes. On Emerald grounds, strategic phrase emphasis happens through scale and line break, not through colour.
- Body copy on Emerald grounds is set in Soft Paper, not Warm Charcoal — Warm Charcoal is too close to Emerald in temperature to read well on it.
- Warm Mid-Grey is used minimally for subtle structure.

The brand does not introduce a separate "dark mode palette." The same six colours, in inverted relationships.

---

### 4j — What's deliberately not in the palette

A list of permanent absences. Each one is a decision, not an oversight.

- **No pure white.** Soft Paper is the background.
- **No pure black.** Warm Charcoal is the body ink. Emerald is the headline ink. Pure black is corporate, sterile, and out of step with the brand's warm-platform register.
- **No additional accent colour.** No clay, no terracotta, no coral, no contrasting warm tone. The two-greens system does the emotional range that an accent would, and the Mint phrase highlight handles deliberate emphasis.
- **No "marketing palette" of brighter or expanded colours.** No campaign-specific colours, no seasonal palettes.
- **No tints, shades, or opacities of the six core colours.** No "Emerald Light," no "Mint at 60%," no "Warm Charcoal at 70% opacity." If a designer needs less weight from a colour, the answer is a smaller size, more space, or a different colour role — not a tint. Lighter Mint is not a tint of Mint; it is its own named role.
- **No gradient colours.** Gradients on type, gradients on backgrounds, gradients on CTAs — none appear in the brand. Gradients are a visual signature of category competitors HMI is built to refuse.
- **No colour-based hierarchy in type, with one exception.** Headlines are Emerald, body is Warm Charcoal. Within either, no further colour-based emphasis — except the Mint strategic phrase highlight (Section 4d), which is governed by strict rules.
- **No additional *expressive* greens.** The brand has three green tones — Emerald, Mint, and Lighter Mint — but only two are expressive (Emerald and Mint). Lighter Mint is a surface tone, not an expressive colour, and is governed by surface rules. The brand does not add a fourth green, an extra Emerald variant, or any "darker Mint" / "brighter Mint" for visual interest.
- **No earthy or nature-coded colours.** No clay, no terracotta, no ochre, no forest tones. The brand is financial, not natural.

---

### 4k — Accessibility

The palette clears WCAG AA accessibility standards comfortably:

- **Warm Charcoal (#1A2B22) on Soft Paper (#F6F7F4):** contrast ratio approximately 13.8:1. The default body-copy pairing — the colour every customer reads in long form. Comfortably exceeds AAA at every size.
- **Warm Charcoal on Lighter Mint (#D4E8B5):** contrast ratio approximately 11.3:1. The body-copy-on-cards pairing. Comfortably exceeds AAA at every size, meaning Lighter Mint cards and panels can hold full body copy without contrast concerns.
- **Emerald (#0A4B34) on Soft Paper:** contrast ratio approximately 9.4:1. The headline pairing. Exceeds AAA for normal text.
- **Emerald on Lighter Mint:** contrast ratio approximately 7.7:1. The headline-on-card pairing. Exceeds AAA for large text and AA for normal text.
- **Soft Paper on Emerald (reversed):** identical contrast ratio of approximately 9.4:1.
- **Warm Charcoal on Mint (#A8D5B4):** contrast ratio approximately 9.1:1. The body-on-soft-section pairing. Exceeds AAA.
- **Emerald on Mint:** contrast ratio approximately 6.2:1. The headline-on-soft-section pairing, and the colour of text behind the Mint phrase highlight. Above AAA for large text and above AA for normal text — fully readable at any size.
- **Lighter Mint on Soft Paper:** contrast ratio approximately 1.2:1. Intentionally very low. This is a surface-on-surface relationship, never a type relationship — Lighter Mint exists as a tonal layer, not as anything the reader needs to distinguish at distance.
- **Warm Mid-Grey on Soft Paper:** intentionally low-contrast. Used for structural elements (dividers, borders, page furniture) where low contrast is the correct visual choice, never for text content that the user needs to read.

Final hex values may be tuned within 1-2 percentage points during implementation to optimise screen rendering, but the named system is fixed.

### 4l — Summary

The palette in one paragraph:

> Soft Paper is the ground, on every surface. Emerald is the primary — used for the wordmark, all headlines, the rare primary button, and the structural elements of diagrams. The brand announces itself in Emerald. Warm Charcoal is the Body Ink — used for every paragraph the customer actually reads. The brand recedes into Warm Charcoal so the words can be read at length. Mint is the secondary expressive green — used as a deliberate warm soft-section and as the one exception to the "no colour-based hierarchy" rule, in the form of a phrase highlight on the brand's locked strategic vocabulary. Lighter Mint is the gentle tonal surface — used for cards, content groupings, hover states, and *most* CTA buttons across the platform; the brand defaults to quiet Lighter Mint buttons because Principle 2 ("Help is offered, never imposed") rules out the pressure of loud CTAs. Warm Mid-Grey is the structural neutral, used for dividers, borders, and page furniture only. There is no white in the palette, no black, no expanded marketing palette, no tints or shades, no gradients, no earthy or nature-coded tones. The discipline is six colours, used in seven clear roles (Lighter Mint does two jobs — tonal surface and secondary button), with one deliberate signature move — the Mint phrase highlight — that gives the brand its recognisable typographic personality.

## Section 5 — Photography and Imagery

*Photography is the highest-stakes brand decision left in the system, and where the property category is at its weakest. The chain's photography is a well-defined library: drone shots of suburbs, suited handshakes, glass-and-chrome offices, smiling families on green lawns. HMI's photography is recognisable by what's absent from it as much as by what's present.*

*The brand's photographic posture: editorial, documentary, warm, and human. Real Australians in real environments — not models in styled sets, not stock in any form.*

---

### 5a — The subject

**Primary subject (70%): real customers in real environments.**
The everyday Australians the platform serves, photographed as themselves, in their own homes and workspaces, making real decisions. Their properties appear in the frame — as the settings their lives happen inside, not as the subject of the photograph. A family gathered in their living room, someone reviewing a contract at their kitchen table, a couple having a conversation on their deck. The human is always the subject; the property is the context that surrounds them.

**Secondary subject (20%): partners and platform people.**
The trusted experts the platform vouches for, captured in editorial portrait style. Considered, dignified, environmental. Not corporate headshots. Not "team photo" energy.

**Tertiary subject (10%): contextual details.**
The objects and spaces of real property investing — a contract on a desk, a whiteboard mid-conversation, a notebook with handwritten numbers. Used sparingly, between human moments.

**What HMI doesn't photograph as the subject of an image:** properties themselves. No exterior hero shots, no "listing-style" interiors, no drone shots of suburbs, no "for sale" imagery. HMI does not sell property. HMI sells capability. The photography reflects that.

---

### 5b — The direction

**Documentary, not staged.** Real moments, witnessed rather than directed. Compositions feel considered but not constructed.

**Environmental, not isolated.** People are shown in their actual contexts. Properties, kitchens, workspaces appear as the natural settings of real life — never as the subject of the frame.

**Natural light.** Window light, daylight, golden-hour light. No studio lighting, no commercial product-style lighting. The light of a real Australian afternoon, not the light of a marketing shoot.

**Editorial, not commercial.** Closest reference: long-form journalism photography (The Monthly, Monocle, NYT Magazine). Furthest reference: real estate marketing photography.

**Restrained post-production.** Consistent subtle warmth across the library, natural skin tones, minimal retouching. No filters, no vintage looks, no heavy grading.

---

### 5c — Casting and styling

**Real customers, not models.** When HMI photographs a customer, that customer is the actual person who used the platform.

**Real Australians, real range.** Age range mid-20s to late-50s. Geographic diversity (suburban Melbourne, regional Queensland, outer Sydney, Newcastle, Perth — not just the inner-city aspirational suburbs). Real demographic diversity reflecting who actually invests in Australian property.

**Casting by posture, not stage.** A 26-year-old buying their first investment property and a 46-year-old on their fourth both fit the brief. What unites them is how they engage, not where they are in the journey.

**Real clothes, real spaces.** Subjects wear what they'd actually wear. Properties are shown as they actually are. The brand does not style up subjects, dress sets, or arrange environments for shoots.

---

### 5d — Partner and founder photography

Partners and founders are photographed once or twice a year, in editorial portrait style. Environmental — captured in working contexts rather than against neutral backgrounds. Same direction principles as customer photography: natural light, real environments, restrained post-production. The look is closer to a Monocle profile portrait than to a LinkedIn headshot.

---

### 5e — The don't list

- No stock photography. Ever.
- No properties as the *subject* of an image. Properties are settings, never the focus.
- No suited handshakes, glass-and-chrome offices, or boardroom imagery.
- No lifestyle-aspiration tropes: keys-being-handed-over, couples-on-balconies, gazing-out-windows, hands-on-coffee-mugs.
- No isolated-subject-on-neutral-background headshots.
- No black-and-white photography.
- No heavy filtering, vintage treatments, or aesthetic-of-the-moment grading.
- No AI-generated imagery.
- No celebrity, influencer, or recognisable-personality casting.

---

### 5f — How the brand operates the photography system

- One photographer (or one consistent small team) shoots the entire customer and partner library. Consistency comes from consistency of eye.
- Photography is commissioned editorially — one subject at a time, taking real time — not in bulk shoots.
- Customer photography is treated as editorial content, not as testimonial decoration. A customer portrait deserves a full page, not a thumbnail beside a star rating.
- The library is built over 12-18 months. The brand accepts the slower pipeline as the cost of having photography that's recognisably its own.

## Section 6 — Illustration and Diagrams

*Information design is a primary brand asset for HMI, not a supporting one. The brand's narrative is structural — "the chain isn't the only way" only lands when the chain itself can be shown — so the visual system has to be capable of making structures visible. Other brands invest in illustration; HMI invests in diagrams. The two are not the same.*

*The brand has no illustration system. No editorial header art. No decorative graphics. No abstract concept imagery. The visual toolkit is three systems: typography, photography, and information design. Anything that doesn't belong to one of those three doesn't belong to the brand.*

---

### 6a — Diagrams, not illustration

The brand makes a hard distinction:

A **diagram** explains a real thing. Its elements correspond to elements in the world — fees, flows, layers, decisions, numbers. The diagram's job is to make a structure visible.

An **illustration** decorates an idea. Its elements are aesthetic rather than informational — a stylised house, a thoughtful figure, an abstract geometric shape "evoking" property. The illustration's job is to make a page feel designed.

HMI uses diagrams. HMI does not use illustration.

This is permanent. There is no narrow exception for editorial header art, no carve-out for long-form essays, no exception for "moments of warmth." Where another brand would commission an illustration, HMI either uses a diagram, uses photography, or uses typography at scale. The brand's three visual systems are the complete toolkit.

---

### 6b — The visual logic of an HMI diagram

Every diagram in the system shares the same behaviour:

**Built from typography, not from imagery.** The primary visual content of a diagram is words. Labels, values, relationships. Plus Jakarta Sans does all of this — body weight for labels, smaller sizes for source citations and metadata. Shapes are minimal — they exist to organise the words, not to decorate them.

**One idea per diagram.** Same discipline as body copy. If a diagram is trying to show three things, it's three diagrams.

**Reads directionally.** Top-down or left-to-right. Property investing involves flows of money, information, and decisions — diagrams visualise those flows with structure. Circular and "ecosystem" diagrams imply equality between elements; directional diagrams imply hierarchy, which is what the brand is actually showing.

**Uses only the brand's palette.** Emerald for primary diagram elements — the boxes, the labels, the connecting lines, and the markers that signal where fees are taken. Warm Charcoal for any body-length text inside or beneath the diagram (descriptions, captions, explanatory paragraphs). Warm Mid-Grey for thin structural rules between elements and for source citations. Mint as a soft-section background where a diagram needs to sit inside an inviting context. Soft Paper as the ground. No additional palette for categorisation. If something needs to be visually distinguished, it's distinguished by position, size, or label — not by an added colour.

**Labels everything directly. No legends.** A diagram that requires a legend is a diagram working too hard. Every element is named on the diagram itself.

**Shows the source.** Where a diagram includes numbers, the source appears within the diagram in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular at body small size, in Warm Mid-Grey — smaller than the data but never hidden. Principle 4 applied to information design.

**Breathes.** Empty space around the diagram is generous. A diagram never fills the canvas to its edges.

---

### 6c — The two diagram families

The system has two distinct families, each doing different work.

#### Family 1 — Structural diagrams

The diagrams that visualise systems: the layers of intermediation, the flow of fees, the architecture of a property transaction, the difference between the chain and the platform. The work of Pillar 2 (The Chain).

**Style:**
- Built from rectangles, horizontal lines, and labels.
- Each element is a labelled box. Two conventions are permitted: Emerald fill with Soft Paper text, or Soft Paper fill with Emerald text and a thin Warm Mid-Grey border. A diagram uses one or the other, never both within the same image.
- Flows between elements are thin Warm Mid-Grey lines with simple arrows. No decorative arrowheads. No curved flourishes.
- Where a fee or clip is taken, a small filled Emerald circle sits outside the labelled box, on the same horizontal axis. The marker reads as a structural call-out — differentiated from the box itself by position (outside the layer) and shape (a circle, not a rectangle), rather than by colour.

#### Family 2 — Quantitative diagrams

The diagrams that show real numbers: fee breakdowns, return calculations, scenario comparisons, partner cost transparency, customer outcomes. The work of Pillar 3 (The Playbook) and Pillar 4 (The New Generation).

**Style:**
- Tables before charts wherever possible. A well-laid-out table in Plus Jakarta Sans, with clean column alignment and considered colour use, is more transparent than a stylised bar chart.
- Where charts are necessary: simple bar charts and line charts only. No pie charts. No donuts. No 3D. No exotic chart types.
- Numbers set in Plus Jakarta Sans, right-aligned in columns where applicable. Layout discipline does the work a monospace would otherwise do.
- Every value visible on the chart itself. No "hover to reveal" as the only way to see a number.
- Axes always start at zero. Scale is never manipulated for visual effect. Honest charts only — Principle 4 at the technical level.
- Source and date appear on every quantitative diagram, in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular at body small size, in Warm Mid-Grey — smaller than the chart's labels but always visible.

---

### 6d — The signature motif: the chain as a vertical stack

The chain — the brand's central narrative metaphor — has one canonical visual form, used repeatedly across the content library until it becomes brand-recognisable on its own.

**The form:**
- A vertical stack of labelled boxes.
- "Knowledge" sits at the top of the stack.
- "The everyday investor" sits at the bottom.
- The layers of intermediation sit between, each one a labelled box.
- Thin Warm Mid-Grey lines connect each layer to the next.
- Each layer that takes a clip has a small filled Emerald circle positioned outside its box, on the same horizontal axis.

**The platform's alternative:**
- Where relevant, shown beside the chain stack as a parallel image.
- Same starting point (knowledge), same ending point (the investor).
- A single direct line between them.
- The intermediating layers are absent.
- No clip-markers (no clips).

**What stays consistent across uses:**
- The vertical architecture.
- The top-to-bottom flow from knowledge to investor.
- The labelled-box construction.
- The Emerald clip-markers positioned outside the layers that take fees.
- The pairing of chain-and-platform as comparative images.

**What varies:**
- The number of layers (depending on what's being shown).
- The labels on each box (depending on what part of the industry is being illustrated).
- The position of clip-markers (wherever the relevant fee is taken).
- The orientation (mostly vertical, occasionally horizontal where layout demands).

This is a visual logic, not a template. The chain-stack appears in essays, social posts, founder content, partner decks, advertising, and educational material — varying with context, but recognisable across every use. Over time, the brand can publish a single chain-stack image with no copy and the audience will understand what it means.

---

### 6e — The don't list

Permanent prohibitions for information design:

- No isometric illustrations of buildings, cities, or "ecosystems."
- No abstract geometric shapes used as decoration in headers, section breaks, or backgrounds.
- No "infographic" style — oversized numbers, decorative icons, illustrated characters explaining concepts.
- No icons within diagrams unless functionally necessary. A house icon labelled "property" is decoration; the word "property" alone does the job.
- No animated diagrams as the default. Animation is used only when it reveals information (e.g., a flow being traced step-by-step). Motion for its own sake is decoration.
- No 3D representations of anything.
- No hand-drawn elements meant to feel "approachable." Approachability lives in tone, not in informal visual cues.
- No charts with manipulated scales, non-zero axes, or visualisations that emphasise aesthetics over readability.
- No commissioned "data-vis as art" pieces.

---

### 6f — What's deliberately not in the system

- **No custom icon library.** Where icons are functionally needed in UI (search, close, download), the brand uses a clean open-source set (Lucide or Tabler, outline weight) sized to match Plus Jakarta Sans's optical weight. No commissioned brand icon set.
- **No patterns or textures.** The brand has no decorative pattern library, no background texture, no watermark graphic.
- **No signature animation language.** Where motion is used, it's functional. There's no "brand motion identity."

---

### 6g — How the system operates

- The brand maintains a template library of reusable diagram constructions — chain-stacks, fee tables, flow diagrams, comparison frames. Designers and content creators draw from the library rather than reinventing each time. This is how consistency is preserved.
- Every published diagram is produced to the same standard as published body copy. If timeline doesn't allow it, the post or essay runs without a diagram — the writing carries the work alone.
- Every diagram is auditable. Where data is shown, the underlying source is documented internally so any claim can be defended on request. This is what protects Principle 4 from drifting into "looks transparent but isn't."
- Diagrams are built to be recyclable. A diagram designed for a long-form essay should be liftable into an Instagram carousel, a founder post, or a partner deck with minor adaptation. A diagram that only works at one size or in one context has been built wrong.

---

### 6h — Summary

The information design system in one paragraph:

> HMI uses diagrams, not illustration. Every diagram is built from typography (Plus Jakarta Sans at the right weights, sizes, and colours for each role), uses only the brand's six colours, labels everything directly, shows its sources, and reads directionally. The system has two families: structural diagrams that visualise systems, and quantitative diagrams that show real numbers. The chain-as-a-vertical-stack — with knowledge at the top, the everyday investor at the bottom, the layers of intermediation between, and small Emerald clip-markers positioned outside the layers where fees are taken — is the brand's signature visual motif, used repeatedly across the content library until it becomes recognisable on its own.

## Section 7 — Layout and Composition

*Layout is where the design principles meet the reader's eye. Three of the five principles are doing direct work in this section: restraint (P1), showing the work (P4), and customer as hero (P5). Layout determines whether the brand feels considered or improvised — the decisions here are quieter than typography or colour, but they're the ones the reader experiences most physically.*

---

### 7a — The foundational logic

Three principles govern every HMI layout:

**1. Space is a primary design element, not what's left over after content.** The brand's pages, slides, social posts, and emails are deliberately less full than category convention. Where another brand fills the page edge-to-edge, HMI lets the edges breathe. The whitespace is doing brand work.

**2. The page reads as a sequence of single ideas, not as a composition of many.** The voice rule "one idea per sentence" has a layout equivalent: one idea per section. A section is allowed to be long, but it's allowed to be about one thing. Pages, posts, and decks don't try to communicate three things in a single screen.

**3. Left-aligned by default. Always.** Centred type is ceremonial. Justified type is corporate. Right-aligned type is for specific tabular contexts only. The default — ragged-right, left-aligned, reading naturally the way English is written — is the most plain-spoken and modern register the brand can sit in.

---

### 7b — The grid

A grid is not visible to the reader, but it's the thing that makes a page feel considered rather than improvised.

**Web — 12-column grid.**
Standard 12-column grid for digital surfaces, with generous gutters between columns. The brand does not use full 12-column-width content for any reading surface. Body text and most editorial content sits in a narrower column — typically 6-8 columns wide on desktop — even when the page width could support more.

**Print and decks — 12-column or 6-column grid, depending on format.**
Same principle. Content sits in a narrower column than the page width permits.

**Maximum content widths (digital):**
- Body copy column: 640-720px wide. Beyond this, line lengths become uncomfortable and contradict the "one idea per sentence" voice rule.
- Single-image hero: full-bleed permitted.
- Display headlines: can extend wider than body, up to 900-1000px on desktop.

The principle: **width is restraint, not opportunity.** Just because a screen is 1440px wide doesn't mean the content should be. The narrower content column is on-brand discipline, not technical limitation.

---

### 7c — Spacing

The brand uses a single, consistent spacing scale across all surfaces. Spacing is built on multiples of 8px (digital) or equivalent units in print.

**The scale:**
- **2xs** — 4px — micro-adjustments between very tight elements
- **xs** — 8px — between tightly-related elements (a label and its input)
- **sm** — 16px — between paragraphs within a section
- **md** — 24px — between elements of a card or content block
- **lg** — 40px — between subsections within a page
- **xl** — 64px — between major sections
- **2xl** — 96-128px — vertical breathing room at the top and bottom of pages, between hero sections and content

Most surfaces use four or five values from this scale, not the full eight. The discipline is to pick the spacing level that fits the relationship, and use it consistently across similar relationships.

**The rule:** spacing should be **deliberate at every step**, not improvised. If a designer is choosing between two values, they pick the larger one. Spacious is on-brand. Tight is not.

---

### 7d — Hierarchy and pacing

How an HMI surface paces its content from top to bottom:

**Pacing convention for a typical page:**
1. **Generous top space** — the page does not start at the top edge. Top margins on hero sections are large.
2. **A single confident headline** — sized at H1 or Display level. Not flanked by other elements competing for attention.
3. **Breathing room beneath the headline** — significant space before the next element. The headline gets to land before the next idea arrives.
4. **One supporting element** — typically a single line of subhead, or a single button, or a single short paragraph. Not three.
5. **A clean break before the next section** — using the xl spacing value at minimum.

The reader should be able to scroll an HMI page and feel the rhythm — one idea, breathing room, next idea, breathing room. Pages that pack content tightly read as "trying to convince you." Pages that breathe read as "already certain."

**For long-form content (essays, brand bible pages, founder posts):**
- Body copy column locked at 640-720px wide.
- Generous leading (1.6 line height, locked from Section 3).
- Paragraphs separated by `sm` spacing (16px). No indentation, no extra ornamentation.
- Subsection breaks separated by `lg` spacing (40px), often with a thin Warm Mid-Grey rule between them.
- Pull-quotes set in larger size of the same body face — never decorative serif, never italicised for effect.

---

### 7e — Composition

**Asymmetry over symmetry.** Symmetrical compositions read as ceremonial or institutional. The brand's compositions are deliberately asymmetric — content sits left, breathing room sits right (or vice versa). The asymmetry is calm, not jarring.

**Strong horizontal rules, minimal vertical separators.** Where sections need to be visually divided, the brand uses thin horizontal Warm Mid-Grey rules — not boxes, not cards, not background colour changes. Sections feel like consecutive paragraphs in an essay, not like discrete tiles.

**Minimal use of cards or boxes.** Cards (rounded rectangles containing grouped content) are the visual signature of modern SaaS dashboards and fintech UI. The brand uses them only where they're functionally required (a UI list of distinct items, a quantitative diagram needing containment). For editorial content, marketing pages, and most surfaces — content sits directly on the Soft Paper ground, separated by space and rules, not by cards.

**Single-column over multi-column where possible.** Multi-column layouts force the eye to make decisions about reading order. Single-column layouts don't. The brand prefers single-column even where the surface could support more — because single-column reads as plain-spoken and considered. Two-column layouts appear where there's a comparative reason (e.g. the chain shown beside the platform), not for visual variety.

---

### 7f — Corner radius

Every rectangular element in the brand has rounded corners. This is uniform across the system — buttons, inputs, cards, image frames, modals, partner badges, content containers. No sharp 90° corners appear anywhere except on full-bleed page sections and on horizontal rules.

Rounded corners are the brand's warmth made small. Sharp corners read as institutional and engineered; rounded corners read as considered and human. The rounding is felt rather than announced — medium, not exaggerated.

**The corner radius scale:**

| Element | Radius |
|---|---|
| Small UI elements (tags, badges, inline pills) | **6px** |
| Buttons | **10px** |
| Form inputs | **10px** |
| Cards and content containers | **14px** |
| Large containers (modals, hero image frames, image crops, photography) | **20px** |
| Full-bleed coloured sections (Emerald background panels) | **0px** |

**Rules:**

- All photography is presented with rounded corners at the 14-20px scale (depending on size). Consistency matters — photography that's rounded on the homepage and sharp on a landing page contradicts the unified warmth of the system.
- Full-bleed coloured sections (a Emerald section that runs to the edge of the page) are not rounded. Rounded corners on full-bleed elements look like floating tiles, not page sections.
- The corner radius scale is fixed. Designers do not improvise new radius values — they pick the appropriate scale level for the element they're working with.
- Border-radius is never animated or transitioned for decorative effect.

---

### 7g — Surface-specific behaviour

A short note on how the system applies across different surfaces:

**Web pages.** Maximum content width 720px for body, up to 1000px for display headlines. Generous top and bottom margins. Single-column primary, multi-column only for comparative content or grids of customer/partner profiles.

**Long-form essays and Brand Bible pages.** Tighter column (640-680px) for reading comfort. Subsection rules between sections. Pull-quotes used sparingly, set in body face at larger size.

**Marketing landing pages.** Same restraint applied. Hero section with a single headline and a single CTA. Content sections paced with `xl` spacing between them. The page should feel like a quietly confident editorial article that happens to have a sign-up at the end — not like a conversion-optimised funnel.

**Social media (Instagram, LinkedIn).** Single-idea-per-post discipline. Headlines set large, with breathing room. The brand's social posts read as more spacious than category convention — which is itself part of the visual signature.

**Decks and partner-facing material.** Same principles. One idea per slide. Display-sized headlines. Generous margins. Content does not extend to slide edges.

**Email.** Plain, generously-spaced layouts. Body copy in a narrow column. Display headlines for important moments. The brand's emails read more like a well-designed letter than a marketing template.

---

### 7h — What the brand doesn't do

A list of permanent layout prohibitions:

- No edge-to-edge content. Margins are always present.
- No multi-column layouts on body copy. Body text sits in a single column.
- No content "tiles," "cards," or "boxes" for editorial content. Reserved for UI and quantitative tables only.
- No centred body copy or centred long-form text. (Centred display headlines are permitted in specific brand-moment contexts — used sparingly.)
- No background colour changes between sections to differentiate them. Sections are differentiated by space and thin rules.
- No "above the fold" pressure. Pages are allowed to require scrolling. Cramming content above the fold contradicts the spaciousness principle.
- No sidebars on body content. Reading content is single-column, end of story.
- No popup overlays, modal interruptions, or "exit-intent" pattern overlays. These contradict the brand's customer-as-hero principle and treat the visitor as a conversion rather than as a person.

---

### 7i — Summary

The layout system in one paragraph:

> HMI layouts are spacious, left-aligned, and single-column by default. Body text sits in a 640-720px column even on wide screens. Spacing is built on an 8px scale; the discipline is to pick the larger value when in doubt. Every rectangular element has rounded corners — 6px on small UI, 10px on buttons and inputs, 14px on cards, 20px on large containers and photography. Sections are separated by space and thin Warm Mid-Grey rules, not by cards or background changes. Pages are paced with generous margins and breathing room — one idea, space, next idea, space. The whitespace is doing brand work, not waiting to be filled.

---

## Section 8 — Voice-in-Design

*Voice-in-design is the section most brand guidelines skip entirely, and it's the one that compounds the most over time. Big decisions (type, colour, layout) are made once and live in master files. Small decisions (the label on a button, the text on an empty state, the message after a form submission) get made hundreds of times by dozens of people — and if there's no guidance, they drift into category-default territory immediately.*

*The voice rules from Section 8 of the narrative bible — make the claim, then back it; one idea per sentence; verbs of agency over adjectives of aspiration — apply at the interface layer too. This section translates them.*

---

### 8a — The five rules of voice-in-design

#### Rule 1 — Buttons say verbs.

Buttons describe the action the user is about to take. The verb is specific to the action, not a category placeholder.

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| Get Started | Start here |
| Learn More | Read this / See how this works |
| Submit | Send / Save / Confirm |
| Sign Up | Create your account |
| Continue | Next step / Review your details |
| Click Here | (delete — buttons are inherently clickable) |

The test: a button's label should read like a sentence completion. "I want to ___." If the verb doesn't fit that sentence, it's wrong.

A second test: if the button label could appear on any other brand's website, rewrite it.

---

#### Rule 2 — Forms ask plain questions.

Form fields ask the user a question the way a person would ask it — not the way a database field would label it.

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| First Name * | What's your first name? |
| Email Address | What email should we use? |
| Password | Choose a password |
| Annual Income | What's your annual income? |
| Investment Goals | What are you trying to achieve? |

Required-field asterisks are not used. Where a field is required, the field itself is required and the validation handles it. Cluttering every label with `*` signals "we're a form" rather than "we're asking."

Placeholder text inside fields is used sparingly. Where placeholders appear, they show an example of the answer ("e.g. Newcastle"), not a repeat of the question.

---

#### Rule 3 — Empty states have a voice.

Empty states are the screens or sections that appear when there's no content yet — an empty inbox, a blank library, a "no results" page. They're invisible to most brands. They're a real opportunity for HMI.

The empty state explains what would normally be here and gives the user a clear next step. It does not apologise, performatively-cheer, or pretend the emptiness is exciting.

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| No results found! | We couldn't find anything matching that. Try a different term, or browse the topic library. |
| You haven't saved anything yet 😊 | This is where the articles, lessons, and resources you save will live. Once you save one, you'll see it here. |
| Oops! Nothing here. | When you start a conversation with a partner, it appears here. |
| Your inbox is empty. | Nothing here yet. When a partner replies, you'll see it here. |

The test: an empty state should feel like a calm note from someone who's been here before, not like a marketing prompt.

---

#### Rule 4 — Errors are honest.

When something goes wrong, the brand acknowledges it directly, without apologetic flourish, without blame, and without hiding what happened.

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| Oops! Something went wrong. | Something on our end broke. We're looking at it. |
| An error has occurred. | The form didn't save. Try again, or reload the page. |
| Invalid input | This needs a valid email address. |
| Please correct the errors below. | Two fields need fixing. They're highlighted below. |
| 404 Not Found | This page doesn't exist. Here's where you might have been heading. |

The brand does not say "oops." The brand does not use exclamation marks in error messages. The brand does not blame the user ("you entered..."). The brand also does not over-apologise. Errors are stated, what to do next is stated, the user moves on.

---

#### Rule 5 — Confirmations match the weight of the action.

When something happens, the confirmation message matches how much the user cares about it.

For small actions: a quiet acknowledgement.
- "Saved."
- "Sent."
- "Updated."

For meaningful actions: a clear statement of what just happened and what comes next.
- "Your account is set up. You'll receive an email with your next steps shortly."
- "Your message is on its way to your partner. They typically reply within a day."

For significant actions: the same plain register, with no celebration.
- HMI does not say "Congratulations!" when a user completes an action. It does not animate confetti. It does not exclaim. The brand treats meaningful moments with the weight they deserve, which means stating them clearly — not performing them.

---

### 8b — Loading and waiting states

Loading states are micro-moments that most brands waste on a generic "Loading..." spinner. HMI's loading states are brief, specific, and on-voice.

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| Loading... | Pulling this together... |
| Please wait | Just a moment |
| Processing... | Working on it |
| Submitting... | Sending |

Where the action is specific enough, the loading state names what's happening — "Fetching the latest figures," "Saving your changes." Where the action is generic, the loading state stays short and plain.

The brand does not use playful loading messages, jokes, or "fun facts while you wait." The register is calm, not playful.

---

### 8c — Notifications and alerts

When the brand interrupts the user — a banner, a toast, a notification — the interruption is justified, brief, and clearly dismissible.

**Three rules:**

1. **The notification has a real reason to exist.** Cookie banners, promotional pop-ups, "rate us!" prompts, and most marketing interruptions don't pass this bar.
2. **The notification states its information and stops.** No additional copy, no "click here for more," no upsell attached.
3. **The notification can be dismissed in one action.** Always.

Notifications are styled in the brand's neutral colour or Soft Paper with Emerald text, not in a warning-coloured banner. The visual register is "calm note," not "urgent alert."

---

### 8d — What the brand never says

A list of words and phrases that don't appear in HMI's interface, regardless of context:

- "Oops!" — and any variant. The brand doesn't say oops.
- "Awesome!" / "Amazing!" / "Wonderful!" — and any variant. The brand doesn't perform enthusiasm.
- "Welcome aboard!" / "Welcome to the team!" — the brand doesn't onboard, doesn't have a team to join.
- "We value your input." / "Your feedback matters." — corporate placeholder phrases.
- "Sorry for the inconvenience." — over-apologetic and meaningless. State what's happening and what comes next.
- "Please" — used sparingly. "Please enter your email" is fine in moderation; "please" at the start of every form field is unnecessary.
- "Click here," "Tap here," "Press this button" — buttons are inherently interactive; describing them as such is filler.
- Exclamation marks in interface copy. Reserved for genuine excitement (which the brand rarely has) and rhetorical writing in long-form content.
- Emojis in product interface copy. Reserved for specific social media contexts where they're earned.

---

### 8e — The plain-language test

The single test every piece of interface copy is auditable against:

**Would a thoughtful adult, asked to write this clearly and briefly, write what's currently here?**

If the answer is "no, they'd write something simpler," rewrite it. If the answer is "yes, this is roughly what they'd say," it's on-brand.

Most interface copy fails this test because it was written by a designer or developer using category-default placeholders, or by a marketer trying to perform brand personality. The discipline is to write interface copy the way you'd write a message to a friend who's a capable adult: clearly, briefly, without performance.

---

### 8f — Summary

Voice-in-design in one paragraph:

> Buttons say verbs that complete the sentence "I want to ___." Forms ask plain questions the way a person would. Empty states explain what would normally be here and what to do next. Errors state what happened and what to do, without "oops" or apology. Confirmations match the weight of the action without performing celebration. The interface sounds like a thoughtful adult wrote it — because one did. The brand never says oops, awesome, welcome aboard, or sorry for the inconvenience. The discipline is plain language at every interaction layer, not just in the hero copy.

## Section 9 — Applications

*The earlier sections specify the toolkit. This section specifies how the toolkit applies to the surfaces HMI operates on. Each subsection states the posture for one surface and includes a worked example. The examples are the most operationally useful part of the document — they're what designers reach for when they're actually building.*

---

### 9a — What the platform is

Help Me Invest is a platform for **education**, **partner introductions**, and **transparency**.

- **Education.** The platform teaches every part of the property investing journey openly — articles, lessons, frameworks, calculators, explainers. The four content pillars (The New Rules, The Chain, The Playbook, The New Generation) describe both the brand's content and the platform's educational architecture.
- **Partner introductions.** When a customer wants help with a specific decision, the platform introduces them to a vetted partner — chosen on merit, paid transparently, aligned with the customer's outcome.
- **Transparency.** The platform makes visible what the chain hides — partner pricing, how each partner gets paid, what's included in HMI's clip and what isn't, the criteria HMI uses to vouch for each partner.

The platform is not a property listings site. Not a transaction platform. Not a marketplace. Customers do not browse properties, watchlist properties, or transact on HMI.

A general writing principle that applies to every surface: **partners are described by what they do for the customer, never by what they are in the industry.** "A partner who handles this part of the process" — not the name of any profession.

If a piece of design implies functionality the platform does not have, the design is off-brand.

### 9h — Governance

The brand belongs to the partnership. Foundational decisions (Sections 1-4) rest with the partner group, not with any individual designer or contractor.

Sections 1-4 are near-permanent. Sections 5-9 flex as the brand grows. The document is reviewed annually with one question per section: *has anything started to feel wrong?* Where yes, update. Where no, the section stands.

When a decision doesn't fit cleanly: check against the five Design Principles (Section 1), then against the Anti-Brand (Section 12 of the narrative bible), then partner-group decision. New precedents are added back into this document.

The master files — wordmark artwork, compact mark, type licenses, colour values, photography library, diagram templates, deck templates — live in a single source-of-truth folder. No one re-creates brand assets from scratch.
