# Help Me Invest. Brand Guidelines.

This document specifies the visual identity. Wordmark, colour, typography, photography, information design, layout, motion, voice-in-design, and applied surfaces. It is the source of truth. If a piece of design can't be justified by something in here, it doesn't ship.

---

## Section 1. Design Principles

Five principles govern every visual decision HMI makes. Each is action-guiding (a designer should be able to audit any draft against any of them and get a clear answer), and each is specific to HMI rather than the kind of principle that could be lifted onto any brand. Every later section, and every piece of design produced under this brand, is auditable against these five.

They are the visual equivalent of Section 8 of the Brand Narrative Bible. Where the narrative voice rule says "make the claim, then back it," the design rules here say "make the page do one thing, then let it breathe." The voice and the design are doing the same work in different mediums.

---

### Principle 1. The brand earns trust by making things simpler, not by making them more polished.

HMI does not perform competence through visual effort. It demonstrates competence by needing less. The brand earns trust the same way the narrative does: by saying one thing clearly, at the right size, with nothing around it. Polish is what brands add when they don't trust their content to stand on its own. HMI trusts its content.

**Consequences:**
- Whitespace is a primary design element, not a leftover.
- Every page, every screen, every social asset does one job. If it's doing three, we redesign.
- We resist the urge to "fill the space." Empty space signals confidence. Crowded space signals a brand that's trying to convince you.
- No decorative flourishes, gradients, glows, drop shadows on type, or design elements added "to make it pop." If a sentence or image isn't strong enough to stand alone, the answer is to make it stronger, not to dress it.

**The test:** *Could we remove 30% of what's on this surface without losing the message?* If yes, we usually should.

---

### Principle 2. Help is offered, never imposed. Every interaction leaves the customer in charge of the decision.

The narrative bible commits HMI to standing alongside the customer, never in front. The design system has to embody that relationship at every surface. The customer is the leader of their own property journey. HMI provides the knowledge, the access, and the trusted experts. The choice, the timing, and the commitment always belong to the customer. The interface is the visual proof of this.

**Consequences:**
- No high-pressure UX patterns. No countdown timers, no fake scarcity, no "only 3 left," no "23 people are looking at this right now."
- No pre-checked consent boxes, no dark patterns, no opt-outs hidden in fine print.
- Help is presented as an option, not as the path. CTAs say "see how this works" or "start with the fundamentals," not "claim your spot" or "don't miss out."
- The customer can always go back, change their mind, or proceed without the help being offered. The architecture supports the customer's autonomy at every step.
- Partner introductions are framed as invitations, not as recommendations the customer needs to follow. The customer evaluates the partner. The partner does not evaluate the customer.

**The test:** *If a customer left this page without taking the action we're suggesting, would they still feel respected and informed?* If no, the page is pushing. Redesign so it offers instead.

---

### Principle 3. Refuse the visual vocabulary of traditional property marketing (gold, navy, drone shots, suited handshakes, glass towers).

The narrative bible commits HMI to refusing the *language* of the chain (financial freedom, savvy investor, exclusive opportunity). The design system has to refuse the *visuals* of the chain with the same discipline. Every visual trope that signals "traditional property marketing" is an asset for them and a liability for us. This principle names the prohibitions plainly so there is no ambiguity about what's off-limits.

**Consequences:**
- No gold. No royal blue or corporate navy. No italic-serif "premium" wordmarks.
- No drone shots of suburbs or skylines. No aerial property photography.
- No suited professionals shaking hands. No glass-and-chrome offices. No boardroom imagery.
- No "lifestyle aspiration" photography. No couple looking out a window, family on a lawn, woman holding coffee on a balcony, keys-in-hand, sold-sign imagery.
- No property exteriors as hero imagery. Property is not what HMI sells. Capability is.

**The test:** *Could this design appear on the website of a traditional property marketing operator?* If yes, push it further until the answer is no.

---

### Principle 4. Make every fee, every source, and every decision visible, even when not legally required.

The narrative bible's transparency commitment ("show everything") has a visual obligation. HMI shows what the chain hides: pricing, incentives, how value moves through the system. The design has to make that information *legible*, not just *present*. When HMI shows a fee structure, the design has to make it readable, not impressive. When HMI explains how a partner is paid, the breakdown has to be the clearest version of that explanation that exists anywhere, not the prettiest.

**Consequences:**
- Fee breakdowns are designed to be audited, not consumed. Clean column alignment, every number visible, every source cited.
- Source citations appear on every page that includes a number, fact, or claim. Warm Mid-Grey at body small size if structural. Emerald at body size if the customer should verify it themselves.
- No "click to reveal" tricks where the disclosure is buried. If something matters, it's visible by default.
- Partner payment arrangements, platform incentive structures, and pricing rationale are documented openly on the platform, not just in legal disclosures.
- Information design is a primary brand asset, not a secondary craft. We invest in diagrams the way other brands invest in photography.

**The test:** *Would a customer who's been burned before look at this and feel they can verify what's being said?* If no, push the transparency further.

---

### Principle 5. The customer's question, decision, or next step is the hero of every surface. The brand isn't.

The narrative bible is explicit: the customer is the hero of every story HMI tells. The design system has to honour that. The brand's visual identity is the architecture beneath the customer's experience, not the protagonist of it. HMI is not "look at us." HMI is "we built this for you." What's specifically the hero here is the actual content of the customer's engagement with the platform: their question, their decision, their next step. Not their face. Not their imagined lifestyle.

**Consequences:**
- The wordmark is small, confident, and used sparingly. The brand never repeats its own name decoratively across surfaces.
- The hero of any page is the customer's question, decision, or next step. Not a brand statement.
- Customer-story content gets editorial-grade treatment. Brand-about-itself content gets minimal treatment.
- Photography centres real customers and real partners, not abstract brand iconography or "HMI as concept" imagery.
- We do not create "celebrating our own milestones" content. The brand's success is the customer's success, visualised through them.

**The test:** *In this design, who is the subject? If the answer is HMI, redesign so the answer is the customer.*

---

### How these principles relate to each other

The five work as a system, not a list. Principle 1 (restraint) is how HMI is confident. Principle 2 (help offered, never imposed) is how HMI honours the customer's autonomy. Principle 3 (refuse the category's visual vocabulary) is how HMI is recognisable. Principle 4 (show every fee and source) is how HMI is trustworthy. Principle 5 (customer's question as hero) is how HMI stays disciplined about who the brand is for.

A design that satisfies one principle but violates another is off-brand. A homepage that's beautifully restrained but centres HMI's logo and tagline above all else fails P5. A fee breakdown that's transparent but uses gold and navy styling fails P3. A partner introduction page that's well-designed but uses urgency language to push the customer forward fails P2.

When in doubt, read the principles in order. Principle 1 is the most fundamental, Principle 5 the most context-specific. None of the five is optional.

## Section 2. Logo and Wordmark

The logo system is the most-repeated piece of brand asset HMI owns. It appears on every email signature, every social avatar, every favicon, every header and footer, every partner deck. The decisions in this section are deliberately conservative. The logo's job is to be quietly correct everywhere, not to be loud anywhere.

The system has three marks: a primary wordmark, a paired "h" lockup, and a compact "h" mark. All three are drawn from the same typeface family. There is no abstract symbol, no metaphor, and no iconographic device in the identity. The "h" mark is a typographic letterform, not an icon. The three marks are one identity at three sizes.

---

### 2a. The primary wordmark

The wordmark is the brand 95% of the time. It appears in every context where the full three words can be read clearly: website headers and footers, marketing materials, decks, email signatures, partner-facing documents, advertising, founder posts.

**Form:**
- The three words "help me invest" set in the brand's primary humanist sans (Plus Jakarta Sans, specified in Section 3).
- All lowercase. Lowercase puts the wordmark in the same conversational register as the brand's voice: plain-spoken, adult-to-adult, stating rather than announcing. Sentence case reads as a corporate proper noun. Uppercase reads as an institution. Lowercase reads as a brand confident enough to write the way it talks.
- Set on a single line as the default. Stacked variants are available where horizontal space is constrained. The single-line lockup is the canonical form.

**Construction:**
- "help me" is set in a heavier weight than "invest". The weight differentiation puts the customer's imperative ("help me") forward and lets "invest" anchor it. This is a single, deliberate typographic decision that gives the wordmark its signature without adding any decorative element.
- "help me" is Plus Jakarta Sans **Bold (700)**. "invest" is Plus Jakarta Sans **Medium (500)**. The weight step is visible. The wordmark carries confident presence on every surface where it appears.
- The wordmark should read as one continuous statement, with the imperative felt forward.
- Letter spacing, word spacing, and proportions are fixed and specified in the master files. The wordmark is never re-typed. It is always used as an artwork file.

**Why this construction:**
- It honours the strength of the name (Principle 1, restraint).
- The lowercase casing matches the brand's plain-spoken voice and the customer-led posture of the narrative (Principle 5).
- The weight differentiation dramatises that posture without saying a word. Design doing the work the name already does.
- It refuses every visual instinct of the category (Principle 3). No symbol, no italic-serif "premium" cue, no abstract corporate mark, no announcing-itself capitalisation.

---

### 2b. The "h" mark and paired lockup

A typographic "h" derived from Plus Jakarta Sans Bold serves as the brand's compact mark and, when paired with the wordmark, as the canonical header and footer lockup.

**Form:**
- A lowercase "h" set in Plus Jakarta Sans Bold, in Emerald on Soft Paper grounds, or Soft Paper on Emerald grounds. The letterform is the mark. No enclosure, no frame, no shape behind it. The "h" sits on the same baseline as the wordmark when paired.
- The "h" is sized to match the cap height of the wordmark when used in a paired lockup, and scales independently when used alone.

**The paired lockup ("h" + "help me invest"):**
This is the canonical header and footer mark across the website, partner decks, and most surfaces where the brand identifies itself. The "h" sits to the left of the wordmark with a small fixed gap, reading as a single composed mark. The pairing makes the brand recognisable at small sizes (where the "h" carries the identity before the wordmark resolves) without sacrificing the wordmark's plain-spoken voice at the size most people will read it.

**The compact "h" alone:**
Used only where the paired lockup cannot be read clearly. Its job is to extend the brand's reach into spaces the wordmark cannot fit, not to compete with the wordmark for attention.

- Favicons.
- App icons.
- Social media avatars.
- Watermarks on partner-facing material.
- Any context where the full wordmark or paired lockup would render too small to be legible.

**Where the "h" is not used:**
- Never as a decorative graphic element in marketing copy or layout. The compact mark replaces the wordmark. It does not supplement it inside body content.
- Never re-typed inline in body copy. When the brand is referenced in writing, the proper name is "Help Me Invest" or, in informal contexts, "HMI" set in the standard body typeface. Not the compact mark as a typographic element.
- Never set in uppercase. Uppercase "H" reads as a corporate monogram (BBC, JPM, EY) and imports exactly the institutional cue the brand is built to refuse.

---

### 2c. Clear space and minimum sizes

All three marks require breathing room. Crowded logos signal a brand trying too hard. Logos with space around them signal a brand that doesn't need to.

**Clear space:**
- Around the wordmark or paired lockup: a minimum margin equal to the height of the lowercase "e" in "help" on all sides. No other text, image, or graphic element may enter that clear space.
- Around the compact "h" alone: a minimum margin equal to the height of the "h" on all sides.

**Minimum sizes:**
- Wordmark or paired lockup: 120px wide (digital) / 30mm wide (print). Below this size, switch to the compact "h" alone.
- Compact "h": 16px (digital) / 8mm (print). Below this size, the mark is not used.

These are minimums, not targets. In most applications the mark should be larger than the minimum. Never so large that it competes with the page's primary content for attention (Principle 5).

---

### 2d. Colour application

The three marks have three permitted colourways:

1. **Emerald on Soft Paper ground.** The default. Used wherever possible.
2. **Soft Paper on Emerald ground.** The inverse. Used on dark backgrounds, photography overlays, and contexts requiring contrast against a coloured ground.
3. **Warm Mid-Grey on Soft Paper ground.** A reduced version used for embossing, single-colour print, partner co-branding, and contexts requiring restraint.

The marks are never set in Mint or Lighter Mint. They are never multi-colour. "help me" and "invest" are always the same colour even though they are different weights. The "h" in the paired lockup is always the same colour as the wordmark beside it.

---

### 2e. What the logo is not, and what it never does

A short list of permanent prohibitions. These exist to protect the system from the kind of drift that compounds over years.

- The logo does not contain an abstract symbol, icon, or graphic device. The "h" is a letterform, not an icon.
- The logo is not modified, restyled, or redrawn for special occasions, campaigns, or partner co-marketing. The wordmark is the same wordmark in every context.
- The logo is not animated for the sake of animation. If the wordmark moves, the movement has a functional reason (e.g. a logo reveal in a video), not a decorative one.
- The logo is not set in italics, outlined, embossed, or given drop shadows.
- The logo is not stretched, condensed, or rotated.
- The full wordmark is not used as a graphic pattern, repeated across a surface, or used decoratively in layouts.
- The compact "h" is not used as a "branded element" inside body copy or marketing assets. Only as a replacement for the wordmark in compact contexts.

If any of these rules need to be broken for a specific application, the decision goes back to the brand owner, not to the designer on the project.

---

### 2f. Files and governance

Master files are produced in:
- SVG (primary digital)
- PNG at standard sizes (web fallback)
- PDF / EPS (print)
- Single-colour variants for each colourway

All files live in a single source-of-truth folder. The wordmark is never re-typed by anyone. It is always used as a file. If a new application requires a logo variant that doesn't exist, the variant is added to the master folder, not improvised in the project file.

## Section 3. Typography

Typography is HMI's most-used brand asset. Every word the brand says, in every medium, lives inside the typeface system. The decisions in this section are foundational. Every later section of these guidelines, and every piece of design produced under this brand, is calibrated to the type. If the type is wrong, nothing else can save the brand. If the type is right, a lot of other decisions become forgiving.

The system is built on two typefaces, each doing a clearly defined job. A modern humanist sans handles the brand's body voice: body copy, UI, navigation, metadata, data, captions, the wordmark. An editorial serif handles the brand's announcing voice: every display headline, every section headline, every pull quote, every big number. The discipline is fewer decisions, made more confidently, with each typeface used only where it earns its place.

---

### 3a. The typeface system

**The brand uses two typefaces:**

**Plus Jakarta Sans.** The body typeface. A modern humanist sans serif with warm, rounded letterforms. Used for the wordmark, all body copy, all UI, all navigation, all metadata, all captions, all data, all source citations, all small typographic moments. Plus Jakarta Sans sits in the warm-modern-platform register: confident enough to refuse the chain's italic-serif premium cues, warm enough to refuse fintech's clinical coldness. The letterforms have a softness in the lowercase "a," "g," "e," and "o" that makes the typeface feel spoken rather than engineered. This is the brand's voice made visible.

**Newsreader.** The editorial display typeface. A contemporary serif with strong optical-sizing across a wide weight range, designed for on-screen reading at large sizes. Used for every headline at H3 size and above, every pull quote, every display-scale number, and every editorial moment where the brand is announcing rather than reading. Newsreader gives HMI the editorial gravitas the brand needs without committing to the heritage register of older serifs. It reads as "a publication that happens to have a platform," not "a financial institution that happens to have a website."

**Why two typefaces, not one.**
An earlier version of this system used only Plus Jakarta Sans across every role. That version worked, but it under-delivered on what the brand is structurally claiming. HMI is built on the conviction that property investing belongs in the same editorial register as serious journalism, and an all-sans system, however well-handled, signals "SaaS product" before it signals "considered publication." Adding Newsreader to the headline layer does brand work no amount of sans hierarchy could do. The reader registers "this is editorial" before the first word resolves. The two typefaces are not equals. They are split clearly by job: Newsreader announces, Plus Jakarta Sans reads. There is no ambiguity, and no role where a designer has to choose between them.

**No third typeface.** The brand has no monospace, no script, no secondary display face. Data and metadata roles that would traditionally use a monospace are handled by Plus Jakarta Sans treated deliberately: smaller sizes, considered colour, clean column alignment. Editorial gravitas is handled entirely by Newsreader at display scale.

**Source.** Plus Jakarta Sans is available on Google Fonts under the SIL Open Font License. Newsreader is available on Google Fonts under the SIL Open Font License, as a variable font supporting optical sizing (`opsz`), weight, and italic axes. The brand uses the official-distributed files only. No re-cuts, no modifications, no derivatives. Newsreader is always loaded with the `opsz` axis enabled (`font-variation-settings: 'opsz' 72` for display sizes, `'opsz' 36` for smaller H3 sizes) so the letterforms are optically tuned for the size they're rendered at.

---

### 3b. How the wordmark is constructed

The wordmark "help me invest" is set in Plus Jakarta Sans with a specific construction that does not change.

- **"help me"** is set in Plus Jakarta Sans **Bold (700)**.
- **"invest"** is set in Plus Jakarta Sans **Medium (500)**.
- The two words and one phrase are set on a single line with consistent letter spacing and word spacing.
- All three words are lowercase.
- The weight differentiation puts the customer-led imperative forward, with "invest" anchoring it. The wordmark reads as one continuous statement, with confident presence on every surface.

The wordmark is never re-typed by hand. It is always used as a master artwork file.

The compact "h" mark and the "h" in the paired lockup are set in Plus Jakarta Sans **Bold (700)**, lowercase. The wordmark is never set in Newsreader. Newsreader is for the brand's *announcing* voice. The wordmark is the brand's *identity*, and the identity is a sans.

---

### 3c. Headline and display hierarchy

Hierarchy in the brand is built primarily through typeface choice (Newsreader for announcement, Plus Jakarta Sans for everything else), secondarily through size, and through italic within Newsreader for selective phrase-level emphasis. Colour is fixed inside each role (headlines are Emerald, body is Warm Charcoal) and is never used to create emphasis within type, with one preserved exception (the Mint phrase highlight, governed by the rules in Section 4d).

All headline sizes below are screen-first specifications. Print sizes scale proportionally.

**Display (D1).** *For hero statements, marketing campaign frames, and the largest brand moments only.*
- Newsreader, weight 400, optical size 72
- `clamp(56px, 7.5vw, 104px)`. Fluid scale from mobile to large desktop.
- Line height: 0.98
- Letter spacing: -0.03em
- Colour: Emerald (or Soft Paper on Emerald grounds)
- Text-wrap: balance
- Used sparingly. One display headline per page maximum.

**Headline 1 (H1).** *Primary page headline.*
- Newsreader, weight 400, optical size 72
- `clamp(36px, 4vw, 56px)`
- Line height: 1.08
- Letter spacing: -0.025em
- Colour: Emerald

**Headline 2 (H2).** *Section headline.*
- Newsreader, weight 400, optical size 72
- `clamp(34px, 3.6vw, 50px)`
- Line height: 1.08
- Letter spacing: -0.022em
- Colour: Emerald

**Headline 3 (H3).** *Subsection headline, card titles where the card carries an editorial title.*
- Newsreader, weight 400, optical size 36
- 22–30px depending on context (cards trend smaller, standalone H3 trends larger)
- Line height: 1.15
- Letter spacing: -0.02em
- Colour: Emerald

**Headline 4 (H4).** *Minor headline, list section titles, small editorial subheads.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans **SemiBold (600)**
- 18–20px
- Line height: 1.3
- Letter spacing: 0
- Colour: Emerald

H4 is the cross-over rung. Above H4, the brand uses Newsreader. At H4 and below, Plus Jakarta Sans. The split is deliberate: at sizes below ~20px, Newsreader's serifs start to fight rendering on screen, and the editorial-publication signal stops doing useful work. At H4 and smaller, Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold carries the headline role with the same plain-spoken register the rest of the body uses.

Headlines from D1 to H3 are set in Newsreader at **weight 400**, not heavier. The brand's signature is the serif setting itself. Loading it up to weight 600 or 700 imports an authoritative, almost broadsheet-headline register that the brand is built to refuse. Weight 400 lets the serif's editorial confidence carry the page without making it shout.

Headlines are set in **sentence case**. Capital first letter, lowercase everything else. All-lowercase headlines feel mannered at the size headlines run. All-caps headlines feel institutional. The wordmark and the "h" mark are the exceptions that prove the rule. They are always all-lowercase because they are a logo, not a sentence.

---

### 3d. Italic emphasis inside Newsreader headlines

A single, brand-defining typographic move: inside any Newsreader headline, a strategic phrase may be set in italic at a slightly lighter weight, picking out the part of the sentence the brand wants the reader to feel.

- **Roman text in the headline:** Newsreader weight 400.
- **Italic phrase inside the headline:** Newsreader weight 300, italic.

The weight step down combined with the italic gives the emphasised phrase a recognisable lift. Softer in colour, looser in posture, structurally distinct from the roman text around it without shouting. Used well, it does what a colour-based highlight does in other brand systems, with more editorial poise.

**Rules:**
- One italic phrase per headline. Never two.
- Reserved for the brand's locked strategic vocabulary, or for phrases doing the same kind of structural work that the locked vocabulary does. Designers do not italicise whatever feels punchy. (See Section 9b of the Brand Narrative Bible for the locked vocabulary list.)
- Italic is *meaningful emphasis*, not decoration. Italics are never used in body copy for emphasis, never used for stylised "vibey" effect, and never used to italicise proper nouns of works inside Newsreader headlines (the editorial italic role is reserved for the brand's emphasis pattern).
- The italic phrase sits on the same baseline as the roman text. Letter spacing inherits from the headline. No separate visual treatment beyond the weight and style.

Example pattern, inside an H1:

> The chain *isn't the only way.* It was just the only way you could see.

The italic plus lighter weight on "isn't the only way" carries the structural critique without an additional colour move. Compare against the older Mint-highlight approach (preserved in Section 4d for specific surfaces): the italic version is editorial, the Mint version is graphic. Both are part of the brand. The italic is the default. The Mint highlight is selective.

---

### 3e. Body copy

Body copy is where the brand's voice does its most consistent work. Every voice rule from Section 8 of the Brand Narrative Bible (make the claim then back it, one idea per sentence, verbs of agency over adjectives of aspiration) relies on body typography that respects them. Body copy is set in **Warm Charcoal (`#1A2B22`)**, the brand's reading colour, not its identity colour. The brand recedes here so the words can be read at length.

Body copy is always Plus Jakarta Sans. Body is never set in Newsreader. Newsreader is for announcement, and a paragraph set in Newsreader reads as quotation rather than prose.

**Body (default)**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular (400)
- 17px (mobile and desktop)
- Colour: Warm Charcoal (`#1A2B22`)
- Line height: 1.6
- Letter spacing: 0
- Maximum line length: 65–75 characters. Longer lines fatigue the reader and contradict the "one idea per sentence" voice rule.

**Body emphasis (within paragraphs)**
- Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold (600) for emphasis, in the same Warm Charcoal as the surrounding body. Used sparingly. One or two emphasised phrases per paragraph maximum.
- Emphasis is created through weight, not colour. Body-copy emphasis is never set in Emerald or Mint. Switching the brand colours into running text breaks the calm reading surface.
- Italics are not used for emphasis in body copy. Inside running body, italics are reserved for proper nouns of works (book titles, publication names). The editorial italic move (Section 3d) lives inside Newsreader headlines only, not in Plus Jakarta Sans paragraphs.

**Inline links within body copy**
- Set in Warm Charcoal (same as the surrounding body), with a 1px Warm Charcoal underline at 3px offset.
- On hover, the underline thickens to 2px and shifts to Emerald, signalling interactivity without switching the link colour into the body stream.
- Links never use Emerald as their default text colour. Keeping body uniform makes long-form reading comfortable, and the underline carries the link signal cleanly.

**Tertiary CTA link** (used as an inline arrow-link CTA, e.g. *"See the modules →"*, *"How the access works →"*)
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500), Warm Charcoal, 1px Warm Charcoal underline at 3px offset.
- On hover, the colour shifts to Emerald, the underline shifts to Emerald, and the arrow nudges forward 2px.

**Body large.** *For opening paragraphs, lede sentences, or content that needs slightly more presence.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 19–22px
- Colour: Warm Charcoal
- Line height: 1.5

**Body small.** *For captions, footnotes, and contextual annotations.*
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 14–15px
- Colour: Warm Charcoal (or Warm Mid-Grey for purely structural metadata like slide numbers, dates, eyebrows)
- Line height: 1.5

**Fine print and legal**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular
- 12–13px
- Colour: Warm Charcoal, never lightened to grey to be visually de-emphasised.
- Line height: 1.5
- Used for footers, terms, disclosures. Fine print is fine in size, not in importance. The brand never visually buries information it is contractually obliged to disclose. This is Principle 4 (make every fee, every source, and every decision visible) applied to the smallest text in the system.

**One important exception: body copy on Emerald grounds.**
When body copy appears on an Emerald background (hero panels, partner deck covers, dark sections, reverse-out contexts), it is set in Soft Paper (`#F6F7F4`), not Warm Charcoal. Warm Charcoal is too close to Emerald in temperature to read well against it. The rule is simple. On light grounds, body is Warm Charcoal. On Emerald grounds, body is Soft Paper, often at slightly reduced alpha (`rgba(246, 247, 244, 0.9)`) to soften the contrast at small sizes.

---

### 3f. Eyebrows

Eyebrows are the small letterspaced labels that sit above section headlines (*the problem*, *what we do*, *investor stories*). The brand uses eyebrows generously as a section-pacing device, because they signal "you are now in this part of the conversation" without crowding the headline.

**Spec:**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500)
- 13px
- Letter spacing: 0.02em (subtle, not aggressive tracking)
- Colour: Warm Mid-Grey
- Set in **uppercase** as the default for structural section labels ("THE PROBLEM", "WHAT WE DO"), or **lowercase** for editorial surfaces where the eyebrow is functioning as a soft category tag.
- Often preceded by a short horizontal Warm Mid-Grey rule (~32px wide) to anchor the eyebrow visually to the section break.

---

### 3g. UI typography

UI elements have their own specifications because their job is different from body or headline copy. They need to be unambiguous, scannable, and tap-targetable. All UI type is Plus Jakarta Sans, never Newsreader.

**Buttons**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500)
- 15px
- Letter spacing: 0
- Button labels are verbs and short phrases. "Start with the fundamentals," "See the breakdown," "How it works." Not "Learn More" or "Get Started" (Principle 5, voice rules in Section 8 of the narrative bible).
- Primary button label colour: Emerald (on Soft Paper fill) or Soft Paper (on Emerald fill), depending on ground. Secondary button label colour: Emerald (on Lighter Mint fill) or Soft Paper (on transparent fill over Emerald ground). See Section 4f for the full button hierarchy and Section 7f for the pill geometry.

**Form labels**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500)
- 14px
- Form labels are plain questions. "What's your first name?" not "First Name *".

**Form inputs**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular (400)
- 16px (minimum, never smaller on mobile, to prevent zoom-on-focus)

**Navigation**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500)
- 15–16px

**Tags, badges, metadata**
- Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500)
- 12–13px
- Set in sentence case or lowercase, never all-caps. All-caps tags signal "fintech" or "ecommerce". Both are the wrong register for HMI.

---

### 3h. Data, metadata, and structural annotations

Since the brand uses no monospace, data and metadata roles are handled by Plus Jakarta Sans treated deliberately. The visual signal of "this is verifiable, not marketing" is carried through layout discipline, colour, and size, not through a different typeface. Big display-scale numbers in editorial contexts (the "1.8–2.5%" stat, the "5 layers" stat) are the one exception. Those are Newsreader, because they are functioning as display headlines made of digits, not as data points in a table.

**The recipe for any data or metadata role:**

1. **Smaller size than the surrounding body.** Body small (14–15px) or fine print (12–13px), depending on context.
2. **Warm Mid-Grey (`#8B8881`) for purely structural metadata.** Slide numbers, timestamps, version labels, internal references, source citations under tables. The grey signals "this is page furniture, not content."
3. **Emerald for any data the reader needs to verify.** Fee percentages set inline within body, partner credentials, ABN and licence numbers. The brand never lightens verifiable information.
4. **Tabular layout with clean column alignment** for any numerical content. Right-aligned numbers, left-aligned labels, consistent column widths. Layout does the work that monospace would otherwise do.
5. **Plus Jakarta Sans Medium** for emphasis within data tables. Never SemiBold or Bold (which would read as marketing).
6. **Big display-scale numbers in editorial layouts are Newsreader.** When a statistic is functioning as a headline (a single number set at display size as the focus of a section), it follows the Newsreader rules in Section 3c, not the data rules here.

**Specific contexts:**

- **Fee breakdowns and pricing tables.** Plus Jakarta Sans Medium for column headers, Regular for values. Numbers right-aligned in their columns. Source and date appear at the bottom of the table in Body Small, in Warm Mid-Grey if structural, in Emerald if the data itself is the point.

- **Source citations.** "As at May 2026" or "Source: APRA, Q2 2025". Set in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular, Body Small size, in Warm Mid-Grey. Always present where data is shown. Never hidden.

- **Partner credentials and verifiable identifiers.** ABNs, licence numbers, regulatory references. Set in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular at body size, in Emerald, never lightened. These are content the reader can audit.

- **Slide numbers, version labels, document metadata.** Plus Jakarta Sans Medium, smallest size (10–12px), in Warm Mid-Grey. Page furniture.

---

### 3i. Behaviour and rules

Six operational rules that apply across the system.

1. **Hierarchy is built through typeface, then size, then italic-within-Newsreader.** Newsreader announces. Plus Jakarta Sans reads. Sizes pace the announcement. Italic within Newsreader carries emphasis. Colour is fixed inside each role (headlines Emerald, body Warm Charcoal) and is never used to create within-role emphasis, with one preserved exception (the Mint phrase highlight, Section 4d).

2. **Two typefaces, used precisely.** Plus Jakarta Sans does body, UI, navigation, metadata, data, the wordmark, and any headline at H4 or smaller. Newsreader does every headline from H3 up, every pull quote, and any display-scale number functioning as a headline. There is no overlap and no ambiguity.

3. **Generous leading. Comfortable line length.** Body copy has a line height of 1.6 and a maximum line length of 65–75 characters. Crowded type contradicts the brand's voice rule of "one idea per sentence." Spacious type reinforces it.

4. **No decorative typographic treatments.** No drop shadows on type. No outlines. No gradients applied to letterforms. No stretching, condensing, or rotating. No "Word Art." If a sentence needs more visual weight, the answer is a larger size, the italic emphasis move (Section 3d), or a different placement. Not a typographic effect.

5. **Type is left-aligned by default.** Centred type reads as ceremonial and institutional. Justified type reads as corporate and dated. Right-aligned type is reserved for specific tabular contexts (right-aligned numbers in a fee table, for example). The default (ragged-right, left-aligned) reads as plain-spoken and modern. One sanctioned exception: the closing CTA headline at the bottom of long pages may be centred at display scale, as an editorial sign-off. Centring is a brand-moment decision, not a layout default.

6. **Use `text-wrap: balance` on every Newsreader headline.** Browsers that support it (most current evergreen browsers) will balance line breaks across the headline block, preventing orphaned words at the end of a line. This is a small, free improvement to typographic polish across the entire system.

---

### 3j. Loading and fallbacks

Both typefaces are delivered via standard web font loading. The fallback stacks are:

```
font-family: 'Plus Jakarta Sans', -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", system-ui, sans-serif;

font-family: 'Newsreader', Georgia, 'Times New Roman', serif;
```

Web font loading uses `font-display: swap` to prevent invisible text during load. Critical above-the-fold weights are preloaded:
- Plus Jakarta Sans Regular (400), Medium (500), SemiBold (600), Bold (700).
- Newsreader Regular (400) roman and italic, Light (300) roman and italic, with `opsz` variation axis.

Newsreader is always loaded with the variable-font axes enabled. `font-variation-settings: 'opsz' 72` is set on Display, H1, H2, and pull quotes. `'opsz' 36` is set on H3.

## Section 4. Colour Palette

Colour is the second-most-recognisable brand signal after type, and the first thing the eye registers before reading anything. The palette is deliberately small. Six colours, six roles, no expansion. Every additional colour would be another decision a designer could get wrong. The discipline is fewer colours, used confidently.

The palette is built around two greens that do different emotional work. A deep emerald carries the trust and gravitas the brand needs as a financial education platform. A soft mint carries the warmth and approachability the brand needs to feel like it's on the customer's side. The two greens are a conversation between expertise and approachability: the same brand at two emotional temperatures.

The palette refuses three category conventions: the gold-and-navy of traditional Australian property, the electric-blue-and-black of modern fintech, and the green-and-clay earthy register of wellness-adjacent brands. HMI is none of these. The palette signals as much before the type even loads.

---

### 4a. The six-role colour system

The brand has six colours, each with one specific role. The roles are fixed.

| Role | Name | Hex | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ground** | Soft Paper | `#F6F7F4` | All backgrounds. 65–75% of any surface. |
| **Soft surface, gentle** | Lighter Mint | `#D4E8B5` | Card backgrounds, content groupings, hover states, gentle tonal lift. |
| **Soft surface, warm** | Mint | `#A8D5B4` | Full soft sections, partner panels, the strategic phrase highlight. |
| **Primary** | Emerald | `#0A4B34` | Wordmark, headlines, primary UI, primary buttons, accent surfaces. |
| **Body Ink** | Warm Charcoal | `#1A2B22` | Body copy, secondary text, inline links. The reading colour. |
| **Neutral** | Warm Mid-Grey | `#8B8881` | Dividers, borders, structural greys, page furniture metadata, eyebrows. |

That is the entire system.

**Two greens, two soft surfaces, one body ink, one structural neutral.** The brand's two-greens identity is unchanged. Emerald carries the trust, Mint carries the warmth, and the conversation between them is the brand's signature visual move. Lighter Mint is not a third expressive green. It's a *softer* version of the Mint surface role, used where a section needs gentle lift rather than full warmth. Warm Charcoal is not an expressive colour at all. It sits in the same family as the greens (a very dark warm green-black) but it recedes so the type can be read at length. The greens *announce*. The charcoal *reads*.

**There is no accent colour.** Emphasis happens through typeface (Newsreader at headline scale), through size, and through the italic-within-Newsreader move (Section 3d). Lighter Mint is a *surface* colour, not an accent. The Mint phrase highlight is preserved as an option (Section 4d) but is no longer the brand's primary emphasis mechanism. Italic emphasis within Newsreader is.

---

### 4b. Ground: Soft Paper (#F6F7F4)

The dominant background colour. Present on every surface, behind every word, supporting every image. Soft Paper is the single highest-leverage decision in the palette. It's what separates HMI from 95% of the category at first glance.

**Rules:**
- Used as the default background on every surface.
- Never replaced with pure white "to brighten things up." Pure white is not in the palette.
- One exception: very large photographic surfaces (a hero image, a customer portrait) can extend full-bleed without Soft Paper around them. Soft Paper returns the moment the image ends.

**Why this specific tone:**
- Warm enough to refuse the fintech-clinical register that pure white imports.
- Restrained enough to refuse the editorial-rustic register that a more saturated cream would commit the brand to.
- Comfortable next to data, photography, both greens, and typography at every weight.

---

### 4c. Primary: Emerald (#0A4B34)

The brand's identity colour. Emerald is what announces HMI. The wordmark, the headlines, the colour of every element where the brand is making itself visible. It is not the colour for reading. That's Warm Charcoal (Section 4g). Emerald is the colour for declaring.

Emerald is a deep, considered green that reads as trusted-expert finance. Confident enough to anchor the whole system, modern enough to refuse the heritage-advisor register, distinctive enough to give the brand an instantly-recognisable colour identity.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- All wordmark, "h" mark, and paired lockup applications on Soft Paper grounds.
- All Newsreader headlines (Display through H3).
- H4 Plus Jakarta Sans SemiBold headlines.
- **Primary button fills on Soft Paper pages.** See Section 4f for the button hierarchy.
- Type colour on Lighter Mint and Mint surfaces, including secondary button labels and headlines that sit inside soft-section panels.
- The text colour inside the Mint phrase highlight (Section 4d), where used.
- Full-bleed surface colour for hero panels, partner decks, founder content covers, and any section requiring reversed-out type.
- Structural elements of all diagrams. The clip-markers that indicate where fees are taken, connecting elements where they need to carry the brand colour.

Never used for:
- Body copy. Body copy is set in Warm Charcoal, the reading colour. Setting body in Emerald makes the brand colour fight for attention against itself across long paragraphs.
- Decoration or visual interest with no structural purpose.

**Never tinted, lightened, or used at reduced opacity.** If less visual weight is needed, the answer is the Neutral colour or a different colour role, not a lighter Emerald.

**Why this specific tone:**
- Modern emerald rather than forest green. The brand is a financial platform, not a nature brand. Forest greens read as earthy and outdoor. Emerald reads as confident, current, and financial.
- Refuses every category convention. Not navy (banking), not black (fintech), not gold (chain), not nature-coded forest (wellness platforms).
- Carries the trusted-expert lineage of financial green (money, growth, stability) without being heritage-advisor about it.
- Pairs naturally with Mint as a two-green system, where the relationship between the two does the brand's emotional work.

---

### 4d. Secondary: Mint (#A8D5B4)

The warmth and approachability colour. Mint does two specific jobs in the brand. Both deliberate, both governed by strict rules.

**Job 1. Soft-section surfaces.** Mint is the background colour for soft sections that need to feel inviting. Partner-introduction panels, sign-up surfaces, customer-welcome contexts, educational callouts, hover states on secondary buttons. When a part of the page should feel softer and more welcoming than the surrounding Soft Paper, the background is Mint.

**Job 2. The strategic phrase highlight (preserved, not default).** Mint can be used to highlight a short phrase from the brand's locked strategic vocabulary by setting it on a Mint background with rounded corners, visually picking out the brand's thesis within a longer headline. This was the brand's previous signature typographic move and is preserved as an option for surfaces where the italic-within-Newsreader move (Section 3d) wouldn't read. For example, hero cards on Emerald grounds where Newsreader white-on-Emerald already does the editorial work and italic emphasis would be lost, or specimen contexts where the colour-graphic emphasis carries the work better than italic-typographic emphasis. **The italic-within-Newsreader move is now the default emphasis mechanism.** The Mint phrase highlight is reserved for selective surfaces where italic alone is insufficient.

**Rules for the phrase highlight (when used):**

- **Soft Paper grounds only.** The phrase highlight is *only* used on Soft Paper backgrounds. On Emerald grounds, Mint sits too close to Emerald in colour family for the highlight to land. On Emerald grounds, strategic phrase emphasis is achieved through italic-within-Newsreader (Section 3d) or through scale and line break alone.
- **One highlight per surface, maximum.** If the hero uses the highlight, nothing else on the page does.
- **One emphasis mechanism per headline.** If a headline uses the italic-within-Newsreader emphasis (Section 3d), it does not also use the Mint highlight. The two are alternatives, not stacking effects.
- **Only on strategic brand vocabulary.** The phrase must come from a small, curated set of brand-anchored expressions: *"on your own terms," "stand alongside, not in front," "show every fee,"* and a small number of other locked phrases.
- **Only on hero and display-scale headlines.** Never in body copy. Never on UI labels. Never on buttons. Never on captions.
- **Visual treatment is fixed.** Mint background (`#A8D5B4`), Emerald text (`#0A4B34`) remains as the type colour, small rounded corners (~6px) on the highlight, no border, no shadow.
- **Padding must accommodate descenders.** Vertical padding of roughly 0.25 × cap height below the baseline and 0.15 × cap height above the cap line. Horizontal padding of roughly 0.3–0.4 × cap height on each side of the first and last character. The visual reading should be comfortable air around the type, with no letter forms ever touching or being clipped by the background edge.
- **The highlight is part of the type, not a sticker.** It sits on the same baseline, with the same letter-spacing, as the rest of the headline.
- **Line-height consideration.** Headlines containing a phrase highlight need slightly more line-height than ordinary headlines (typically 1.2 instead of 1.08) to prevent the highlight on one line from overlapping the descender or cap of an adjacent line.

**Used for soft-section surfaces:**

- Soft section backgrounds where a part of the page should feel softer and more inviting than surrounding Soft Paper.
- Partner-introduction panels.
- Approachable UI contexts: sign-up panels, getting-started prompts, customer welcome surfaces, friendly callouts.
- Hover states on secondary buttons (Mint background, Emerald text).
- Educational content callouts where an explanation needs to feel different from surrounding body content.

**Never used for:**

- Body copy. Mint is a surface or a phrase highlight, never the colour of running text.
- The wordmark or "h" mark.
- Decoration or visual interest with no structural purpose.
- Buttons or UI elements that need to draw the eye to a primary action. Those are Emerald.
- Arbitrary emphasis. The phrase highlight is reserved for the locked strategic vocabulary only, and only where italic-within-Newsreader is unavailable.

---

### 4e. Soft Surface, Gentle: Lighter Mint (#D4E8B5)

The third tonal layer. Lighter Mint is a softer, lifted version of the Mint surface, used where a section, card, or panel needs gentle lift from the Soft Paper ground without the full warmth of Mint. It is a *tonal* colour, not an expressive one.

Lighter Mint does most of the brand's card and grouping work. Investor story cards, partner cards, the three "what we do" pillars, education module thumbnails, hover surfaces, the chain diagram's labelled boxes. All sit on Lighter Mint. The discipline is that Lighter Mint never tries to do Mint's job, and Mint never tries to do Lighter Mint's job.

Lighter Mint sits between Soft Paper and Mint on the same green family axis. The three together (Soft Paper, Lighter Mint, Mint) give the brand fine tonal control across long pages, allowing visual rhythm without breaking the calm.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- Card backgrounds within Soft Paper pages. Investor story cards, partner cards, content tiles, feature panels. Places where grouped content needs to lift gently off the ground.
- The labelled boxes inside structural diagrams (the chain-stack, the platform comparison).
- Hover and selected states on Soft Paper surfaces. When a row, card, or section becomes interactive, it shifts to Lighter Mint as a soft acknowledgement.
- The fill on secondary buttons used on Soft Paper grounds.
- Quiet section panels. Where a long page needs gentle tonal rhythm, sections of Lighter Mint can be alternated with Soft Paper to pace the reading.
- Educational callouts and quotes that should feel gathered but not warmly emphasised.
- Form field backgrounds in inviting contexts (sign-up forms, welcome flows), where a Mint background would feel too warm but Soft Paper would feel inert.
- The investor-stories editorial lede block on long-form pages (Lighter Mint panel behind the section opener with a large italic Newsreader em-dash ornament).

Never used for:
- The strategic phrase highlight. Lighter Mint sits too close to Soft Paper to do the highlight's job.
- Headlines, body copy, or any running type. Lighter Mint is a surface colour only. When type appears *on* Lighter Mint (card content, callouts), the type is set in Emerald (headlines) or Warm Charcoal (body).
- The wordmark or compact mark.
- Backgrounds where full Mint warmth is required.

---

### 4f. The button hierarchy

The brand has three button styles, each with a specific role. The geometry of all buttons is the **pill** (fully rounded ends, `border-radius: 999px`). See Section 7f for the corner radius system and why buttons specifically use the pill shape.

**Primary button.**
The strongest visual call on a page. Used for *the* one action the brand most wants the customer to take in a given context, and only used when the page genuinely has one such action. One per surface maximum.

Two contextual variants:

1. **On Emerald grounds** (the hero panel, dark sections): Soft Paper fill, Emerald text. The button reads as a bright pill against the dark green field.
2. **On Soft Paper grounds** (the closing CTA, secondary pages): Emerald fill, Soft Paper text. The button reads as a dark anchor against the light field.

Use for: "Start with the fundamentals" on the homepage hero, "Create your account" on sign-up flows, "Send to my partner" on partner-introduction screens, "Save" on settings-with-real-consequences. Anywhere the action is the page's single point of focus.

**Secondary button.**
The quieter CTA. Used when the action is exploratory, optional, or one of several equally weighted choices. Often paired with a primary button on the same page (e.g. "Start with the fundamentals" plus "How it works"). Frequently used *alone* on content surfaces where the brand wants to offer the action without pushing it.

Two contextual variants:

1. **On Emerald grounds.** Transparent fill, Soft Paper text, 1px Soft Paper border. A ghost pill, equal in geometry to the primary, quieter in weight.
2. **On Soft Paper grounds.** Lighter Mint fill, Emerald text. Hover shifts to Mint fill.

Use for: "How it works," "Read the breakdown," "Compare your options," "Browse partners," "Continue reading." Most CTA moments on the platform are secondary buttons, not primary.

**Tertiary text link.**
The quietest call. Used for non-action interactions: inline CTAs within editorial content, navigational links, "See the modules →," "Read Aisha's story →," "View details," "Back to all partners."

Spec: Plus Jakarta Sans Medium (500), Warm Charcoal, with a 1px Warm Charcoal underline at 3px offset. Hover shifts colour to Emerald and the underline to Emerald with 2px thickness. Often paired with a trailing arrow that nudges forward 2px on hover.

**The discipline:**

Designers audit a layout by asking two questions.

1. *Does this page have a single most-important action?* If yes, that action gets the primary button. Nothing else on the page does. If no (the page is exploratory, informational, or offering multiple equally-weighted paths), all CTAs are secondary buttons or tertiary links.

2. *Is the loudest button the action the brand most wants?* If a less-important action is rendered as primary, the page is pushing the wrong thing. Demote it.

The brand defaults toward quiet buttons. This is intentional. Restraint as the brand's CTA register. Loud CTAs are a brand pushing. Quiet CTAs are a brand offering. HMI is in the second category.

---

### 4g. Body Ink: Warm Charcoal (#1A2B22)

The reading colour. Warm Charcoal is the colour of every long-form sentence the customer encounters: body copy in paragraphs, secondary text in captions, lede paragraphs beneath headlines, the body of UI panels. It is not a brand-announcing colour. Its job is to recede enough that the type can be read at length without the brand colour fighting for attention against itself.

Warm Charcoal is a very dark warm green-black. It sits in the same family as Emerald (both share a warm green undertone) but its colour temperature pulls toward neutral so it reads as comfortable reading text rather than as the brand colour repeated.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- Body copy in every context: homepage, education content, partner introductions, founder posts, email body, anywhere paragraphs are read.
- Secondary text: captions, footnotes, supporting paragraphs.
- Body of UI panels and cards where the user is reading content, not interacting with controls.
- Inline links *within body copy* (rendered with an underline; the underline carries the link signal, not a colour change to Emerald, so the body remains comfortable to read).
- Tertiary text-link CTAs ("See the modules →" or "Read Aisha's story →").
- Labels inside the Lighter Mint boxes of the structural chain diagram.

Never used for:
- Headlines or display copy. Headlines are Emerald.
- The wordmark. The wordmark is Emerald.
- Buttons or primary UI actions. Those are Emerald-filled with Soft Paper text (or Soft Paper-filled with Emerald text, depending on ground).
- Anywhere the brand should *announce* itself rather than recede.

**Why this specific tone:**

- Dark enough to read comfortably at body sizes across long paragraphs.
- Warm enough to harmonise with the Emerald/Mint family. Not a neutral grey, not a cool charcoal.
- Recessive enough that Emerald headlines and buttons read as the *announcements* on a page, with the body settling beneath them as readable content.
- Not pure black. Pure black is corporate, sterile, and out of step with the brand's warm-platform register.

---

### 4h. Neutral: Warm Mid-Grey (#8B8881)

A single mid-grey with a warm undertone, used for genuine structural greyness. Never for de-emphasising content.

**Rules:**

Used for:
- Hairline dividers and rules between sections (including the short eyebrow-rule that precedes a section eyebrow).
- Form input borders and table borders.
- Disabled UI states.
- Eyebrows. The small letterspaced labels above section headlines (see Section 3f).
- Connecting lines and arrows inside structural diagrams.
- "CLIP TAKEN" and "DIRECT ACCESS" annotation labels in the chain diagram.
- Secondary metadata labels (dates, timestamps, slide numbers, customer story name-plates like "Aisha · 26 · outer Melbourne").
- Captions beneath data tables and source citations that are structural rather than verifiable (e.g. *"Source: APRA, Q2 2025"*, *"As at May 2026, indicative range across major capitals"*).

Never used for:
- Body copy or "secondary" text. Secondary text is set in Warm Charcoal at a smaller size.
- Fine print, footnotes, or legal text. These are set in Warm Charcoal, not lightened. Fine print is fine in size, not in importance.
- De-emphasising any content the customer needs to read.

**Why a warm grey, not a cool one:**

- Cool greys (with blue undertones) read as fintech and clash with the warm ground.
- A grey with subtle warm undertones sits naturally next to Soft Paper, both greens, and Warm Charcoal. Reading as part of the same family rather than as a clinical out-of-system colour.

---

### 4i. How the colours work together

The system is built so each colour has a clear lane. A well-designed HMI surface uses:

- Soft Paper as the ground (always).
- Emerald for the wordmark, headlines (in Newsreader), and the rare primary button (always).
- Warm Charcoal for body copy and reading-length text (always).
- Lighter Mint as a gentle tonal lift for cards, content groupings, hover states, and most CTA buttons on Soft Paper pages (frequently).
- Mint for full soft-section surfaces and, selectively, the strategic phrase highlight (rarely, on Soft Paper grounds only).
- Warm Mid-Grey for genuine structure only. Dividers, eyebrows, structural metadata (rarely).

A surface that uses all six colours equally is over-designed. A well-balanced HMI surface might be 60% Soft Paper, 15% Warm Charcoal (body copy is most of the words), 8% Emerald (headlines, wordmark, buttons), 10% Lighter Mint (cards or content groupings), 6% Mint (a soft section or a highlighted phrase, where used), 1% Warm Mid-Grey. Those proportions are the discipline.

**The two-greens relationship.**

The brand's most distinctive visual move is the conversation between Emerald and Mint. When the two appear on the same surface (Emerald as the headline colour, Mint as a soft section behind it or as the highlight on a strategic phrase), the brand reads as both confident and warm at once. Warm Charcoal sits beneath the conversation as the calm reading layer. It does not participate in the two-greens move.

**Reverse contexts (Emerald as ground).**

When Emerald is used as a background (the hero panel, partner deck covers, dark sections), the relationships invert cleanly.
- Soft Paper becomes the headline and primary type colour. Newsreader weight 400 sits as Soft Paper on Emerald, with the same italic-emphasis move (Section 3d) where applicable.
- Mint continues to function as a softer-section surface inside Emerald sections, used to create a layered feel where needed.
- Lighter Mint is used minimally on Emerald grounds. The contrast against Emerald is too soft to do useful tonal work. Where a softer lift inside an Emerald section is needed, use Mint at full saturation instead.
- **The Mint phrase highlight is not used on Emerald grounds.** Emphasis on Emerald comes from italic-within-Newsreader.
- Body copy on Emerald grounds is set in Soft Paper at slightly reduced alpha (~0.9), not Warm Charcoal.
- Buttons follow the inverted scheme in Section 4f.
- Warm Mid-Grey is used minimally for subtle structure (rules, fine metadata).

The brand does not introduce a separate "dark mode palette." The same six colours, in inverted relationships.

---

### 4j. What's deliberately not in the palette

A list of permanent absences. Each one is a decision, not an oversight.

- **No pure white.** Soft Paper is the background.
- **No pure black.** Warm Charcoal is the body ink. Emerald is the headline ink.
- **No additional accent colour.** No clay, no terracotta, no coral, no contrasting warm tone. The two-greens system does the emotional range that an accent would.
- **No "marketing palette" of brighter or expanded colours.** No campaign-specific colours, no seasonal palettes.
- **No tints, shades, or opacities of the six core colours**, with one narrow technical exception (body-copy-on-Emerald uses Soft Paper at ~0.9 alpha for readability; this is a rendering tune, not a new colour). No "Emerald Light," no "Mint at 60%."
- **No gradient colours.** Gradients on type, gradients on backgrounds, gradients on CTAs. None appear in the brand.
- **No colour-based hierarchy in type**, with one preserved exception (the Mint strategic phrase highlight in Section 4d, used selectively on Soft Paper grounds only). The default emphasis mechanism is italic-within-Newsreader (Section 3d).
- **No additional *expressive* greens.** Three green tones exist (Emerald, Mint, Lighter Mint), but only two are expressive (Emerald and Mint). Lighter Mint is a surface tone.
- **No earthy or nature-coded colours.** No clay, no terracotta, no ochre, no forest tones.

---

### 4k. Accessibility

The palette clears WCAG AA accessibility standards comfortably:

- **Warm Charcoal on Soft Paper:** ~13.8:1. AAA at every size.
- **Warm Charcoal on Lighter Mint:** ~11.3:1. AAA at every size. Lighter Mint cards can hold full body copy without contrast concerns.
- **Emerald on Soft Paper:** ~9.4:1. Exceeds AAA for normal text.
- **Emerald on Lighter Mint:** ~7.7:1. Exceeds AAA for large text and AA for normal text.
- **Soft Paper on Emerald:** ~9.4:1.
- **Warm Charcoal on Mint:** ~9.1:1. AAA.
- **Emerald on Mint:** ~6.2:1. Above AAA for large text and above AA for normal. The text-in-phrase-highlight pairing reads at any size.
- **Lighter Mint on Soft Paper:** intentionally very low. A surface-on-surface relationship, never type at distance.
- **Warm Mid-Grey on Soft Paper:** intentionally low. Structural elements only, never text content the user needs to read.

Final hex values may be tuned within 1-2 percentage points during implementation to optimise screen rendering. The named system is fixed.

## Section 5. Photography and Imagery

Photography is the highest-stakes brand decision left in the system, and where the property category is at its weakest. The chain's photography is a well-defined library: drone shots of suburbs, suited handshakes, glass-and-chrome offices, smiling families on green lawns. HMI's photography is recognisable by what's absent from it as much as by what's present.

The brand's photographic posture is editorial, documentary, warm, and human. Real Australians in real environments. Not models in styled sets, not stock in any form.

---

### 5a. The subject

**Primary subject (70%): real customers in real environments.**
The everyday Australians the platform serves, photographed as themselves, in their own homes and workspaces, making real decisions. Their properties appear in the frame as the settings their lives happen inside, not as the subject of the photograph. A family gathered in their living room. Someone reviewing a contract at their kitchen table. A couple having a conversation on their deck. A customer working at a laptop in their backyard. The human is always the subject. The property is the context that surrounds them.

**Secondary subject (20%): partners and platform people.**
The trusted experts the platform vouches for, captured in editorial portrait style. Considered, dignified, environmental. Not corporate headshots. Not "team photo" energy.

**Tertiary subject (10%): contextual details.**
The objects and spaces of real property investing. A contract on a desk, a whiteboard mid-conversation, a notebook with handwritten numbers. Used sparingly, between human moments.

**What HMI doesn't photograph as the subject of an image:** properties themselves. No exterior hero shots, no "listing-style" interiors, no drone shots of suburbs, no "for sale" imagery.

---

### 5b. The direction

**Documentary, not staged.** Real moments, witnessed rather than directed. Compositions feel considered but not constructed.

**Environmental, not isolated.** People are shown in their actual contexts. Properties, kitchens, workspaces appear as the natural settings of real life. Never as the subject of the frame.

**Natural light.** Window light, daylight, golden-hour light. No studio lighting, no commercial product-style lighting.

**Editorial, not commercial.** Closest reference: long-form journalism photography (The Monthly, Monocle, NYT Magazine). Furthest reference: real estate marketing photography.

**Restrained post-production.** Consistent subtle warmth across the library, natural skin tones, minimal retouching. No filters, no vintage looks, no heavy grading. The library carries a quiet green-leaning warmth that sits naturally next to Soft Paper and the brand greens.

---

### 5c. Casting and styling

**Real customers, not models.** When HMI photographs a customer, that customer is the actual person who used the platform.

**Real Australians, real range.** Age range mid-20s to late-50s. Geographic diversity (suburban Melbourne, regional Queensland, outer Sydney, Newcastle, Perth, not just the inner-city aspirational suburbs). Real demographic diversity reflecting who actually invests in Australian property.

**Casting by posture, not stage.** A 26-year-old buying their first investment property and a 46-year-old on their fourth both fit the brief. What unites them is how they engage, not where they are in the journey.

**Real clothes, real spaces.** Subjects wear what they'd actually wear. Properties are shown as they actually are.

---

### 5d. Hero photography (Refit-style split)

The canonical homepage hero pairs an Emerald text panel on the left (~50% of the viewport) with a full-bleed photograph on the right (~50% of the viewport). The two halves meet at a clean vertical edge. The photograph is treated as the editorial subject of the page, not as decoration behind the text.

**Direction for the hero image:**
- A customer in their own environment, framed so the subject reads cleanly within the right-hand half of the frame.
- Natural light. Golden-hour or soft window light. The image carries a warm, green-leaning tonal cast that harmonises with the brand greens.
- The composition is calm. No busy detail in the area closest to the Emerald panel, so the meeting edge between Emerald and photograph reads cleanly.
- No text overlay on the image. No gradient overlay. No soft-focus treatment. The Emerald panel and the photograph meet at a clean vertical edge.
- Image extends full-bleed on its half. No rounded corners on the hero photograph at this scale (see Section 7f for corner rules on full-bleed surfaces).
- On mobile, the split becomes vertical. Emerald panel stacks above the photograph, or the photograph runs full-bleed beneath the Emerald panel. The editorial relationship between text panel and image is preserved.

**Where a single full-bleed photograph spans the whole viewport instead of a split:** the same direction principles apply. The image is composed with a calm, low-detail zone (sky, soft foliage, neutral wall) in the area where text would sit, so the text is readable without overlay treatments.

---

### 5e. Partner and founder photography

Partners and founders are photographed once or twice a year, in editorial portrait style. Environmental, captured in working contexts rather than against neutral backgrounds. Same direction principles as customer photography: natural light, real environments, restrained post-production. The look is closer to a Monocle profile portrait than to a LinkedIn headshot.

---

### 5f. Portrait treatment in card layouts

When customer or partner portraits appear inside card layouts (the three Investor Stories cards on the homepage, the Meet the Partners grid), the portraits are presented as cropped editorial images inside a Lighter Mint card container. The image is rounded at the medium card scale (14–16px corner radius). The card itself uses Lighter Mint as the background, sits with generous breathing room above and below, and contains: the rounded image at the top, a small Warm Mid-Grey metadata line ("Aisha · 26 · outer Melbourne") beneath the image, a single editorial body line in Warm Charcoal (the customer's story summary), and a tertiary text-link CTA at the bottom ("Read Aisha's story →").

---

### 5g. The don't list

- No stock photography. Ever.
- No properties as the *subject* of an image. Properties are settings, never the focus.
- No suited handshakes, glass-and-chrome offices, or boardroom imagery.
- No lifestyle-aspiration tropes: keys-being-handed-over, couples-on-balconies, gazing-out-windows, hands-on-coffee-mugs.
- No isolated-subject-on-neutral-background headshots.
- No black-and-white photography.
- No heavy filtering, vintage treatments, or aesthetic-of-the-moment grading.
- No AI-generated imagery in the final library. (AI may be used in test/wireframe phases. Final hero and library images are commissioned.)
- No celebrity, influencer, or recognisable-personality casting.

---

### 5h. How the brand operates the photography system

- One photographer (or one consistent small team) shoots the entire customer and partner library. Consistency comes from consistency of eye.
- Photography is commissioned editorially. One subject at a time, taking real time. Not in bulk shoots.
- Customer photography is treated as editorial content, not as testimonial decoration. A customer portrait deserves a full page, not a thumbnail beside a star rating.
- The library is built over 12-18 months. The brand accepts the slower pipeline as the cost of having photography that's recognisably its own.

## Section 6. Illustration and Diagrams

Information design is a primary brand asset for HMI, not a supporting one. The brand's narrative is structural. "The chain isn't the only way" only lands when the chain itself can be shown. So the visual system has to be capable of making structures visible.

The brand has no illustration system. No editorial header art. No decorative graphics. No abstract concept imagery. The visual toolkit is three systems: typography, photography, and information design. Plus a single narrow exception for editorial ornaments drawn from Newsreader (Section 6g).

---

### 6a. Diagrams, not illustration

The brand makes a hard distinction.

A **diagram** explains a real thing. Its elements correspond to elements in the world (fees, flows, layers, decisions, numbers). The diagram's job is to make a structure visible.

An **illustration** decorates an idea. Its elements are aesthetic rather than informational (a stylised house, a thoughtful figure, an abstract geometric shape "evoking" property). The illustration's job is to make a page feel designed.

HMI uses diagrams. HMI does not use illustration.

This is permanent. Where another brand would commission an illustration, HMI either uses a diagram, uses photography, or uses Newsreader at scale.

---

### 6b. The visual logic of an HMI diagram

Every diagram in the system shares the same behaviour.

**Built from typography, not from imagery.** The primary visual content of a diagram is words. Labels, values, relationships. Plus Jakarta Sans does all of this. Body weight for labels, smaller sizes for source citations and metadata. Shapes are minimal. They exist to organise the words, not to decorate them.

**One idea per diagram.** Same discipline as body copy. If a diagram is trying to show three things, it's three diagrams.

**Reads directionally.** Top-down or left-to-right. Property investing involves flows of money, information, and decisions. Diagrams visualise those flows with structure. Circular and "ecosystem" diagrams imply equality between elements. Directional diagrams imply hierarchy, which is what the brand is actually showing.

**Uses only the brand's palette.** Lighter Mint for the labelled boxes of the chain (the boxes are content groupings, sitting at the same tonal level as the brand's other cards). Warm Charcoal for the labels inside boxes. Warm Mid-Grey for thin connecting lines between elements. Emerald for the clip-markers (the small filled circles that indicate where fees are taken). Soft Paper as the ground. Body-length text inside or beneath the diagram is Warm Charcoal. Source citations are Warm Mid-Grey at body small size.

**Labels everything directly. No legends.** A diagram that requires a legend is a diagram working too hard.

**Shows the source.** Where a diagram includes numbers, the source appears within the diagram in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular at body small size, in Warm Mid-Grey. Smaller than the data, but never hidden.

**Breathes.** Empty space around the diagram is generous. A diagram never fills the canvas to its edges.

**Reveals on scroll.** The chain diagram and other structural diagrams use a layered reveal-on-scroll animation, with each labelled box fading and rising by 12–16px as it enters the viewport (see Section 6.5, Motion). Each layer is staggered by ~100ms, so the diagram appears to assemble as the reader arrives. The reveal honours `prefers-reduced-motion`. The reveal is functional. It gives the diagram a moment to read as a sequence of layers rather than as a single shape. Not decorative.

---

### 6c. The two diagram families

The system has two distinct families, each doing different work.

#### Family 1. Structural diagrams

The diagrams that visualise systems: the layers of intermediation, the flow of fees, the architecture of a property transaction, the difference between the chain and the platform. The work of Pillar 2 (The Chain).

**Style:**
- Built from rectangles, vertical connectors, and labels.
- Each element is a labelled box with a **Lighter Mint** background (matching the brand's card register), Warm Charcoal label inside, and a thin Warm Mid-Grey border at low opacity (optional).
- Vertical connectors between boxes are thin Warm Mid-Grey lines with simple downward arrows.
- Where a fee or clip is taken, a small filled **Emerald** circle sits **outside** the labelled box, positioned to the right of the box, with a short connecting line and a small Warm Mid-Grey "CLIP TAKEN" label set in Plus Jakarta Sans Medium at 12px, letter-spacing 0.06em, beside it. The marker reads as a structural call-out. Differentiated from the box by position (outside the layer) and shape (a circle, not a rectangle), with Emerald as its colour to make the clip-marker the only Emerald element in the diagram.
- The chain version stacks on the left of the canvas. The platform version sits on the right, with a single direct line from top endpoint to bottom endpoint, no intermediating layers and no clip-markers. Annotated with a small Warm Mid-Grey "DIRECT ACCESS" label.

#### Family 2. Quantitative diagrams

The diagrams that show real numbers: fee breakdowns, return calculations, scenario comparisons, partner cost transparency, customer outcomes. The work of Pillar 3 (The Playbook) and Pillar 4 (The New Generation).

**Style:**
- Tables before charts wherever possible. A well-laid-out table in Plus Jakarta Sans, with clean column alignment and considered colour use, is more transparent than a stylised bar chart.
- Where charts are necessary: simple bar charts and line charts only. No pie charts. No donuts. No 3D.
- Numbers set in Plus Jakarta Sans, right-aligned in columns where applicable. Layout discipline does the work a monospace would otherwise do.
- Big display-scale numbers in editorial layouts (e.g. "1.8 to 2.5%" as a section stat) are set in **Newsreader** at display weight (see Section 3c). Those numbers are headlines made of digits, not data points in a table.
- Every value visible on the chart itself. No "hover to reveal" as the only way to see a number.
- Axes always start at zero. Scale is never manipulated for visual effect. Honest charts only. Principle 4 at the technical level.
- Source and date appear on every quantitative diagram, in Plus Jakarta Sans Regular at body small size, in Warm Mid-Grey.

---

### 6d. The signature motif: the chain as a vertical stack

The chain is the brand's central narrative metaphor. It has one canonical visual form, used repeatedly across the content library until it becomes brand-recognisable on its own.

**The form:**
- A vertical stack of labelled Lighter Mint boxes.
- "The property" sits at the top of the stack.
- "The everyday investor" sits at the bottom.
- The layers of intermediation sit between, each one a labelled box.
- Thin Warm Mid-Grey lines connect each layer to the next with small downward arrows.
- Each layer that takes a clip has a small filled Emerald circle positioned outside the box (to the right), with a short connecting line and a small "CLIP TAKEN" label in Warm Mid-Grey letterspaced metadata.

**The platform's alternative:**
- Shown beside the chain stack as a parallel image on the same canvas.
- Same starting point (the property), same ending point (the investor).
- A single direct line between them.
- The intermediating layers are absent.
- No clip-markers.
- A small Warm Mid-Grey "DIRECT ACCESS" label sits beside the connector, balancing the "CLIP TAKEN" labels on the chain side.

**Both diagrams reveal on scroll.** The chain stack reveals first, layer by layer (~100ms stagger). The platform connector then draws in alongside (~800–950ms after the chain begins), so the comparison reads as a structural answer to the chain rather than as a simultaneous statement.

**What stays consistent across uses:**
- The vertical architecture.
- The top-to-bottom flow.
- The Lighter Mint labelled-box construction.
- The Emerald clip-markers positioned outside the layers that take fees.
- The pairing of chain-and-platform as comparative images.

**What varies:**
- The number of layers (depending on what's being shown).
- The labels on each box.
- The position of clip-markers.
- The orientation (mostly vertical, occasionally horizontal where layout demands).

---

### 6e. The don't list

Permanent prohibitions for information design:

- No isometric illustrations of buildings, cities, or "ecosystems."
- No abstract geometric shapes used as decoration in headers, section breaks, or backgrounds.
- No "infographic" style. Oversized numbers as decoration, decorative icons, illustrated characters explaining concepts.
- No icons within diagrams unless functionally necessary.
- No 3D representations of anything.
- No hand-drawn elements meant to feel "approachable." Approachability lives in tone, not in informal visual cues.
- No charts with manipulated scales, non-zero axes, or visualisations that emphasise aesthetics over readability.
- No commissioned "data-vis as art" pieces.

---

### 6f. What's deliberately not in the system

- **No custom icon library.** Where icons are functionally needed in UI (search, close, download, arrow), the brand uses a clean open-source set (Lucide or Tabler, outline weight) sized to match Plus Jakarta Sans's optical weight.
- **No patterns or textures.** The brand has no decorative pattern library, no background texture, no watermark graphic.

---

### 6g. Editorial ornaments

A narrow exception to "no decoration" exists for typographic ornaments drawn from Newsreader itself. Most prominently the **large italic Newsreader em-dash** that anchors the Investor Stories lede block. This is a Newsreader glyph used at very large size (200–280px), positioned partly outside the frame of a Lighter Mint editorial panel, at low opacity (~12%), reading as page furniture rather than as content. The em-dash signals "editorial" without adding a separate illustration system.

This is the only context in which an em-dash appears in HMI's output. The brand does not use em-dashes anywhere else (not in body copy, not in headlines, not in interface copy). For prose that would conventionally call for an em-dash, use a full stop, a colon, or parentheses instead.

Rules for editorial ornaments:
- Drawn from Newsreader glyphs only. Never custom shapes.
- Used at very low opacity (~10–15%).
- Anchored to a specific surface role (currently the Investor Stories lede block). New ornaments are added with the same deliberation as a new colour role would be.
- Never used as decoration in body copy or UI.

---

### 6h. How the system operates

- The brand maintains a template library of reusable diagram constructions: chain-stacks, fee tables, flow diagrams, comparison frames. Designers and content creators draw from the library rather than reinventing each time.
- Every published diagram is produced to the same standard as published body copy. If timeline doesn't allow it, the post or essay runs without a diagram.
- Every diagram is auditable. Where data is shown, the underlying source is documented internally so any claim can be defended on request.
- Diagrams are built to be recyclable. A diagram designed for a long-form essay should be liftable into an Instagram carousel, a founder post, or a partner deck with minor adaptation.

## Section 6.5. Motion

Motion is part of the system, not a decorative add-on. It honours the brand's restraint (Principle 1) and refuses the high-energy scroll-jacking conventions of modern marketing sites (Principle 3). HMI's motion is subtle, slow, and never demands attention. It simply makes the experience of arriving at a page feel considered.

---

### 6.5a. The motion principles

**1. Functional, not decorative.** Every motion has a reason. Reveals confirm "you've arrived at this section." Hover lifts confirm "this is interactive." Diagram layer staggers give the eye a sequence to follow. Motion that doesn't earn its place is removed.

**2. Quiet, not energetic.** Reveals take 700–1400ms, not 200ms. The brand never pops. It settles in. Easing is `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)`, a slow ease-out that arrives gently. No bounces. No springs. No "delight" micro-interactions.

**3. Honours `prefers-reduced-motion`.** Every animation in the system has a `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)` override that disables transform and opacity transitions. Accessibility is not optional.

**4. Transform and opacity only.** Animated properties are limited to `transform` and `opacity` so motion stays GPU-accelerated and never reflows the page. No animated colours, no animated widths, no animated `top`/`left`.

---

### 6.5b. The four motion patterns

The brand has four sanctioned motion patterns. Anything outside these four is a brand-level decision, not a designer's call.

**1. Section reveal on scroll.**
Sections, eyebrows, headlines, paragraphs, cards, and diagram layers all share a default reveal-state: opacity 0 with a 14–24px downward translateY, transitioning to opacity 1 and translateY 0 when the element enters the viewport. Duration ~1100–1400ms. Each element has an optional `--reveal-delay` for staggering.

**2. Chain diagram staggered reveal.**
The chain-stack and the platform comparison reveal layer-by-layer rather than all at once. Each layer is delayed by ~100ms from the previous one, and the platform comparison line draws in ~800–950ms after the chain stack begins. The reveal is functional. It gives the diagram a moment to read as a sequence of intermediating layers rather than as a single composed shape.

**3. Card hover lift.**
Cards that are themselves anchors (investor profiles, partner cards, education module thumbnails) lift gently on hover. Scale `1.005–1.01`, a small upward translateY (~1–2px), with a 1.2–1.4 second ease-out transition. The lift is barely perceptible at full speed. It signals interactivity without performing.

**4. Hero entrance.**
On initial page load, the hero block fades in from below with a slight staggered cascade across its child elements (eyebrow, headline, lede, CTAs). Total duration ~1100ms. Child elements stagger by ~70ms. Used only on the homepage hero, not on every hero across the site.

---

### 6.5c. What the brand never does with motion

- No scroll-jacking. The page scrolls at the user's pace, always.
- No parallax. The brand does not pretend depth.
- No autoplay video on hero surfaces.
- No marquee/ticker animations.
- No animated emoji, animated illustrations, animated decorative elements.
- No "kinetic typography". Type that animates in word-by-word, letter-by-letter, or with rotating phrases.
- No spring physics on UI elements.
- No animated gradients or shifting background colours.
- No "scroll progress" indicators.

---

### 6.5d. Implementation note

Easing is consistent across the system via a CSS custom property:

```
--ease-out-quart: cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1);
```

Every transition that participates in the system uses this easing. The slow ease-out is what gives the brand's motion its calm. A sharp ease would feel commercial. The slow one feels considered.

---

## Section 7. Layout and Composition

Layout is where the design principles meet the reader's eye. Three of the five principles are doing direct work here: restraint (P1), showing the work (P4), and customer as hero (P5).

---

### 7a. The foundational logic

Three principles govern every HMI layout.

**1. Space is a primary design element, not what's left over after content.** The brand's pages, slides, social posts, and emails are deliberately less full than category convention.

**2. The page reads as a sequence of single ideas, not as a composition of many.** The voice rule "one idea per sentence" has a layout equivalent. One idea per section.

**3. Left-aligned by default. Always.** Centred type is ceremonial. Justified type is corporate. Right-aligned type is for specific tabular contexts only. One sanctioned exception: the closing CTA headline at the bottom of long pages may be centred at display scale, as an editorial sign-off.

---

### 7b. The grid

**Web: 12-column grid** for digital surfaces, with generous gutters between columns. The brand does not use full 12-column-width content for any reading surface. Body text and most editorial content sits in a narrower column, typically 6-8 columns wide on desktop, even when the page width could support more.

**Print and decks: 12-column or 6-column grid, depending on format.** Same principle.

**Maximum content widths (digital), drawn from the prototype source-of-truth:**

```
--col-body:    720px;   /* general body column */
--col-reading: 680px;   /* long-form essays, brand bible pages */
--col-display: 1000px;  /* display headlines */
--col-shell:  1200px;   /* outer page shell */
```

Single-image hero: full-bleed permitted on photographic halves of split heroes, and on the rare full-viewport photographic hero.

The principle: **width is restraint, not opportunity.** Just because a screen is 1440px wide doesn't mean the content should be.

---

### 7c. Spacing

The brand uses a single, consistent spacing scale across all surfaces. Spacing is built on multiples of 8px (digital), drawn from the prototype source-of-truth:

```
--space-2xs:  4px;
--space-xs:   8px;
--space-sm:  16px;
--space-md:  24px;
--space-lg:  40px;
--space-xl:  64px;
--space-2xl: 128px;
```

Most surfaces use four or five values from this scale. The discipline is to pick the spacing level that fits the relationship, and use it consistently across similar relationships.

**The rule:** spacing should be **deliberate at every step**, not improvised. If a designer is choosing between two values, they pick the larger one.

---

### 7d. Hierarchy and pacing

How an HMI surface paces its content from top to bottom:

1. **Generous top space.**
2. **A single confident headline**, sized at H1 or Display level. Not flanked by other elements competing for attention.
3. **Breathing room beneath the headline.**
4. **One supporting element**, typically a single line of subhead, or a single button, or a single short paragraph.
5. **A clean break before the next section**, using the `xl` spacing value at minimum.

The reader should be able to scroll an HMI page and feel the rhythm. One idea, breathing room, next idea, breathing room.

**For long-form content (essays, brand bible pages, founder posts):**
- Body copy column locked at 680px wide.
- Generous leading (1.6 line height).
- Paragraphs separated by `sm` spacing (16px). No indentation, no extra ornamentation.
- Subsection breaks separated by `lg` spacing (40px), often with a thin Warm Mid-Grey rule between them.
- Pull-quotes set in Newsreader at H3–H2 scale (in keeping with the headline rule), Emerald, with the italic-emphasis move (Section 3d) applied where a phrase carries the structural weight.

---

### 7e. Composition

**Asymmetry over symmetry, with one sanctioned centring.** Symmetrical compositions read as ceremonial or institutional. The brand's compositions are deliberately asymmetric. Content sits left, breathing room sits right (or vice versa). The asymmetry is calm, not jarring. The sanctioned exception: the closing CTA at the bottom of a long page may be centred at display scale as an editorial sign-off ("Property investing back in the hands of everyday Australians."). One centred moment per page maximum.

**Strong horizontal rules, minimal vertical separators.** Where sections need to be visually divided, the brand uses thin horizontal Warm Mid-Grey rules. Not boxes, not cards, not background colour changes. Sections feel like consecutive paragraphs in an essay, not like discrete tiles.

**Cards where they earn their place.** The brand uses cards selectively. Investor story cards, partner cards, education module thumbnails, the three "what we do" pillars, the chain diagram's labelled boxes. All use Lighter Mint as their background and a 14–16px corner radius. For editorial running content, cards are not used. Content sits directly on Soft Paper, separated by space and rules.

**Single-column over multi-column for reading.** Multi-column body copy is never used. Three-column card grids are used for distinct, parallel items (three investor stories, three pillars, three partner profiles). Two-column splits appear for comparative reasons (the chain shown beside the platform, the hero's Emerald-panel-and-photograph composition), not for visual variety.

---

### 7f. Corner radius

Every rectangular element in the brand has rounded corners, with one specific exception (pill buttons) and one structural exception (full-bleed coloured sections).

| Element | Radius |
|---|---|
| Small UI elements (tags, badges, inline pills, Mint phrase highlight) | **6px** (`--radius-sm`) |
| Form inputs | **10px** (`--radius-md`) |
| Cards and content containers (investor cards, partner cards, pillar cards, education thumbnails, chain-diagram labelled boxes) | **14px** (`--radius-lg`) |
| Large containers (modals, image frames, image crops, photography in cards) | **20px** (`--radius-xl`) |
| Investor-stories editorial lede block (Lighter Mint panel) | **24px** (a slightly larger custom radius for this specific surface) |
| **Buttons** (all primary, secondary, ghost) | **999px** (fully rounded pill) |
| Full-bleed coloured sections (Emerald hero panel, Emerald deck covers) | **0px** |

**The button pill.**
Every button across the system (primary, secondary, ghost) is a fully rounded pill (`border-radius: 999px`). The pill shape is deliberate. Rectangles with rounded corners read as SaaS-product buttons. Pills read as editorial CTAs. Combined with the brand's restraint principle (no shadows, no gradients, no glowing borders), the pill is what makes a CTA feel like an offer rather than a push.

**Rules:**

- All photography is presented with rounded corners at the 14–20px scale (depending on size). One sanctioned exception: the full-bleed hero photograph and any full-bleed photographic surface.
- Full-bleed coloured sections (an Emerald section that runs to the edge of the page) are not rounded.
- The corner radius scale is fixed. Designers do not improvise new radius values. They pick the appropriate scale level for the element they're working with.
- Border-radius is never animated or transitioned for decorative effect.

---

### 7g. Surface-specific behaviour

A short note on how the system applies across different surfaces.

**Web pages.** Body content sits in a 720px column. Long-form reading content sits in a 680px column. Display headlines may extend to 1000px. The outer page shell is 1200px. Generous top and bottom margins. Single-column primary. Multi-column only for comparative content or grids of customer/partner profiles.

**Long-form essays and Brand Bible pages.** Tighter column (680px) for reading comfort. Subsection rules between sections. Pull-quotes set in Newsreader at H3–H2 scale.

**Marketing landing pages.** Same restraint applied. Hero section with a single headline and one or two CTAs (one primary, one secondary). Content sections paced with `xl` spacing between them. The page should feel like a quietly confident editorial article that happens to have a sign-up at the end. Not like a conversion-optimised funnel.

**Social media (Instagram, LinkedIn).** Single-idea-per-post discipline. Headlines set large in Newsreader. The brand's social posts read as more spacious than category convention. Which is itself part of the visual signature.

**Decks and partner-facing material.** Same principles. One idea per slide. Display-sized Newsreader headlines. Generous margins.

**Email.** Plain, generously-spaced layouts. Body copy in a narrow column. Newsreader display headlines for important moments. The brand's emails read more like a well-designed letter than a marketing template.

---

### 7h. What the brand doesn't do

A list of permanent layout prohibitions.

- No edge-to-edge body content. Margins are always present.
- No multi-column body copy.
- No content "tiles," "cards," or "boxes" for editorial running content. Reserved for UI, quantitative tables, and the brand's specific card patterns (investor stories, partner profiles, pillars, education thumbnails, chain-diagram boxes).
- No centred body copy or centred long-form text. Centred display headlines are permitted in the specific closing-CTA context above, used sparingly.
- No background colour changes between sections to differentiate them. Sections are differentiated by space and thin rules, and selectively by a Lighter Mint or Mint full-section panel where the brand wants a deliberate warm moment.
- No "above the fold" pressure. Pages are allowed to require scrolling.
- No sidebars on body content. Reading content is single-column, end of story.
- No popup overlays, modal interruptions, or "exit-intent" pattern overlays.

## Section 8. Voice-in-Design

Voice-in-design is the section most brand guidelines skip entirely, and it's the one that compounds the most over time. Big decisions (type, colour, layout) are made once and live in master files. Small decisions (the label on a button, the text on an empty state, the message after a form submission) get made hundreds of times by dozens of people. If there's no guidance, they drift into category-default territory immediately.

The voice rules from Section 8 of the narrative bible (make the claim then back it, one idea per sentence, verbs of agency over adjectives of aspiration) apply at the interface layer too. This section translates them.

One overarching rule that runs through everything here: **the brand does not use em-dashes.** Not in body copy, not in headlines, not in interface copy. Where prose would conventionally call for an em-dash, use a full stop, a colon, parentheses, or rewrite the sentence. The em-dash glyph appears in only one place in HMI's output: the large, low-opacity Newsreader em-dash ornament on the Investor Stories lede block (Section 6g). That is a typographic ornament, not punctuation.

---

### 8a. The five rules of voice-in-design

#### Rule 1. Buttons say verbs.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| Get Started | Start with the fundamentals |
| Learn More | Read this / See how this works / How it works |
| Submit | Send / Save / Confirm |
| Sign Up | Create your account |
| Continue | Next step / Review your details |
| Click Here | (delete, buttons are inherently clickable) |

A button's label should read like a sentence completion. "I want to ___." If the verb doesn't fit that sentence, it's wrong. A second test: if the button label could appear on any other brand's website, rewrite it.

---

#### Rule 2. Forms ask plain questions.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| First Name * | What's your first name? |
| Email Address | What email should we use? |
| Password | Choose a password |
| Annual Income | What's your annual income? |
| Investment Goals | What are you trying to achieve? |

Required-field asterisks are not used. Placeholder text inside fields is used sparingly. Where placeholders appear, they show an example of the answer ("e.g. Newcastle"), not a repeat of the question.

---

#### Rule 3. Empty states have a voice.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| No results found! | We couldn't find anything matching that. Try a different term, or browse the topic library. |
| You haven't saved anything yet | This is where the articles, lessons, and resources you save will live. Once you save one, you'll see it here. |
| Oops! Nothing here. | When you start a conversation with a partner, it appears here. |
| Your inbox is empty. | Nothing here yet. When a partner replies, you'll see it here. |

The empty state should feel like a calm note from someone who's been here before, not like a marketing prompt.

---

#### Rule 4. Errors are honest.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| Oops! Something went wrong. | Something on our end broke. We're looking at it. |
| An error has occurred. | The form didn't save. Try again, or reload the page. |
| Invalid input | This needs a valid email address. |
| Please correct the errors below. | Two fields need fixing. They're highlighted below. |
| 404 Not Found | This page doesn't exist. Here's where you might have been heading. |

The brand does not say "oops." The brand does not use exclamation marks in error messages.

---

#### Rule 5. Confirmations match the weight of the action.

For small actions, a quiet acknowledgement.
- "Saved."
- "Sent."
- "Updated."

For meaningful actions, a clear statement of what just happened and what comes next.
- "Your account is set up. You'll receive an email with your next steps shortly."
- "Your message is on its way to your partner. They typically reply within a day."

For significant actions, the same plain register, with no celebration. HMI does not say "Congratulations!" It does not animate confetti.

---

### 8b. Loading and waiting states

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| Loading... | Pulling this together... |
| Please wait | Just a moment |
| Processing... | Working on it |
| Submitting... | Sending |

The brand does not use playful loading messages, jokes, or "fun facts while you wait."

---

### 8c. Notifications and alerts

Three rules.

1. **The notification has a real reason to exist.** Cookie banners, promotional pop-ups, "rate us!" prompts, and most marketing interruptions don't pass this bar.
2. **The notification states its information and stops.**
3. **The notification can be dismissed in one action.** Always.

Notifications are styled in the brand's neutral colour or Soft Paper with Emerald text, not in a warning-coloured banner.

---

### 8d. What the brand never says

- "Oops!" and any variant.
- "Awesome!" / "Amazing!" / "Wonderful!" and any variant.
- "Welcome aboard!" / "Welcome to the team!"
- "We value your input." / "Your feedback matters."
- "Sorry for the inconvenience."
- "Please" used sparingly.
- "Click here," "Tap here," "Press this button."
- Exclamation marks in interface copy.
- Emojis in product interface copy.
- Em-dashes anywhere in brand output (see Section 8 opener).

---

### 8e. The plain-language test

**Would a thoughtful adult, asked to write this clearly and briefly, write what's currently here?**

If the answer is "no, they'd write something simpler," rewrite it.

## Section 9. Applications

The earlier sections specify the toolkit. This section specifies how the toolkit applies to the surfaces HMI operates on.

### 9a. What the platform is

Help Me Invest is a platform for **education**, **partner introductions**, and **transparency**.

- **Education.** The platform teaches every part of the property investing journey openly: articles, lessons, frameworks, calculators, explainers. The four content pillars (The New Rules, The Chain, The Playbook, The New Generation) describe both the brand's content and the platform's educational architecture.
- **Partner introductions.** When a customer wants help with a specific decision, the platform introduces them to a vetted partner. Chosen on merit, paid transparently, aligned with the customer's outcome.
- **Transparency.** The platform makes visible what the chain hides: partner pricing, how each partner gets paid, what's included in HMI's clip and what isn't, the criteria HMI uses to vouch for each partner.

The platform is not a property listings site. Not a transaction platform. Not a marketplace.

A general writing principle that applies to every surface: **partners are described by what they do for the customer, never by what they are in the industry.** "A partner who handles this part of the process," not the name of any profession.

### 9h. Governance

The brand belongs to the partnership. Foundational decisions (Sections 1-4) rest with the partner group, not with any individual designer or contractor.

Sections 1-4 are near-permanent. Sections 5-9 flex as the brand grows. The document is reviewed annually with one question per section: *has anything started to feel wrong?* Where yes, update. Where no, the section stands.

When a decision doesn't fit cleanly: check against the five Design Principles (Section 1), then against the Anti-Brand (Section 12 of the narrative bible), then partner-group decision. New precedents are added back into this document.

The master files (wordmark artwork, "h" mark, paired lockup, type licenses, colour values, photography library, diagram templates, deck templates) live in a single source-of-truth folder.
